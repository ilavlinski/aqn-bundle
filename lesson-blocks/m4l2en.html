Lesson 4.2: Research Papers and Publications

Module 4: Research & Innovation Lab

The first paper I contributed to was terrifying. My PI handed me a draft and said, "You did this work. Now write it down." I stared at the blank page for hours. I had no idea what "methods section" meant or how many figures was too many. I emailed my advisor drafts at midnight. Three months later, that paper was submitted to a journal. A year later, it was published. That publication sits on my CV now, 20 years old, and it still matters because it shows I know how to ask questions, design experiments, and communicate findings. In your research career, papers are your voice. Right now, I'm teaching you how to find the right PI to work with, and then how to speak your research into existence.

VIDEO SCRIPT:

Third, and this is crucial, read their recent papers. Go to Google Scholar, type their name, and read the abstracts of their last five papers. This does two things: it helps you understand what they actually do (not just their job title), and it gives you something specific to mention when you reach out. Never contact a professor saying 'I want to do research.' Instead say, 'I read your 2024 paper on propellant combustion efficiency, and I'm interested in how you modeled thermal degradation in the chamber.'

It's central to being a scientist or engineer.

Finding Your Research PI: The Cold Email Strategy

Step 1: Identify Your Research Interest

Start broad. You don't need to know exactly what you want to do for the next 10 years, you just need a direction. "I'm interested in space propulsion" or "I want to work on climate modeling" or "I'm drawn to robotics for lunar exploration." That's enough to start searching.

Step 2: Find Professors Doing That Work

Use Google Scholar (scholar.google.com). Search your research interest. You'll see recent papers and who published them. Click on authors' names to see their institutional affiliation. Now go to that institution's website and find the professor's faculty page. Look for:

  • Recent publications (last 2-3 years)
  • Lab description or research overview
  • Lab website with contact info
  • Current student or postdoc names (they're hiring if they have recent students)
  • Funding sources listed (NASA, NSF, DOE, these are good signs)

Pro tip: If the professor has many recent papers and a lab website, they're actively running a large group and likely have resources for new members. If they haven't published in two years, they may be on sabbatical or winding down their lab.

Step 3: Read Their Papers (Strategically)

You don't need to understand the whole paper. Read the abstract, the introduction (which explains the "why"), and the conclusion. This takes 15 minutes per paper. After reading 2-3 of their recent papers, you understand their focus and can speak intelligently about it.

What to note: One specific finding or method they use. "I noticed you developed a new calibration method for thermal sensors" or "Your 2023 paper on ice-sheet dynamics introduces a novel grid-refinement approach." When you email, you'll reference this. It shows you actually read their work, not just their title.

Step 4: The Cold Email, Template & Real Example

Template:

Subject: Interest in [Research Topic] Research - [Your Major]

Dear Dr. [Name],

I am a [sophomore/junior/graduate student] in [major] at [university]. I recently read your [year] paper on [specific topic], particularly your approach to [one specific method or finding]. This aligns closely with my interest in [research area]. I am eager to gain hands-on research experience and believe your lab would be an excellent fit. I have experience with [one relevant skill: coding, lab techniques, data analysis], and I'm committed to contributing meaningfully to your group. Would you have time to discuss potential research opportunities?

Thank you for considering my inquiry.
Best regards,
[Your Name]
[Your University]
[Email] [Phone]

Real Example (Earth Science):

Subject: Interest in Climate Modeling Research - Environmental Engineering

Dear Dr. Martinez,

I am a junior in Environmental Engineering at University of New Mexico. I recently read your 2024 paper in Journal of Climate on precipitation-runoff coupling in high-altitude basins, specifically your novel data assimilation framework for sparse observation networks. This connects directly to my interest in water resource modeling for arid regions like northern Peru, where I'm from. I have experience with Python and have completed coursework in hydrodynamics and climate dynamics. Would you have an opening for an undergraduate researcher in your lab, ideally starting this summer?

Thank you for considering my inquiry.
Best regards,
Juan Quispe
University of New Mexico
jquispe@unm.edu | (505) 555-0123

Why this works: You showed you read their specific paper, you named a specific method, you explained why it matters to you (not just "I want research experience"), and you gave them one reason to say yes (you have basic skills). Most PIs get dozens of vague inquiries ("Can I join your lab?"). One that shows actual engagement stands out.

Expect rejection. Most will ignore you or say no. That's not personal. They may not have funding, have a full lab, or be on sabbatical. Send five emails if one receives a positive response, you're ahead of the game. My first PI cold email got rejected. My second got ignored. My third came back with "Come in for an interview." That's the norm.

Once You're In the Lab: The Paper-Writing Path

The Research Timeline

Most projects take 6-18 months from start to publication. You'll typically start as a lab member doing assigned tasks, setting up experiments, collecting data, coding analysis scripts. After a few months, the PI will involve you in interpreting results. After 6-12 months, they'll ask you to draft a section of the paper. After 12-18 months, you're a co-author and your name is on the publication.

The Paper Structure (for STEM research)

Every scientific paper follows the same format (called IMRaD: Introduction, Methods, Results, and Discussion):

  • Abstract (150-250 words): One paragraph. Why the research matters, what you did, what you found, why it matters now. Write this last.
  • Introduction (800-1,200 words): "What is the problem?" Cite prior research. Build up to your research question. Ends with "We therefore investigate..." or "In this paper, we..."
  • Methods (1,000-2,000 words): "How did you do it?" Describe your experiment, instruments, data sources, analysis techniques. Detailed enough that someone else could reproduce your work.
  • Results (1,000-1,500 words + figures): "What did you find?" Present data, charts, statistics. Don't interpret yet; just show what you got.
  • Discussion (1,200-2,000 words): "What does it mean?" Explain your results. Compare to previous research. Acknowledge limitations. Discuss implications.
  • References: Every claim needs a source. Use citation managers like Zotero (free) or Mendeley.

Real Journal Examples to Target (STEM):

  • Journal of Geophysical Research (broad earth science, NASA-friendly)
  • IEEE Transactions on Aerospace and Electronic Systems (engineering-focused)
  • Icarus (planetary science)
  • Nature Geoscience (high-impact earth science)
  • Astrophysical Journal (space science, but highly competitive)
  • Review of Scientific Instruments (experimental methods and instruments)

Conference Papers vs. Journal Papers

Early in your career, you might present at a conference (like AGU, AIAA, or SACNAS, we'll cover this in Lesson 3) before submitting to a journal. A conference paper is shorter (4-6 pages) and has a faster review process (3 months). A journal paper is longer (20-30 pages) and takes 6-12 months for review and revisions. Both count on your CV. Start with conferences to build credibility, then journals for deeper findings.

The Writing Process

Your first draft will be terrible. That's normal. Write freely. Don't edit as you go. Once you have a full draft, your PI will send it back covered in comments. You revise. Repeat 3-5 times. This isn't punishment, it's training. Each revision you learn to explain better, tighten arguments, anticipate questions. By the final draft, you can write clearly.

Common Mistakes I See

  • Too much interpretation in Methods: Methods is just "how," not "why." Save interpretation for Discussion.
  • Figures with no captions: Every figure needs a detailed caption (2-3 sentences) explaining what it shows. Readers should understand it without reading text.
  • Overciting or under-citing: Don't cite every sentence (readers get tired of "according to Smith et al."). Do cite surprising claims, specific data, prior findings you build on.
  • Passive voice overuse: "Data was collected" → "We collected data." Active voice is clearer.
  • No story: A paper should tell a story. "We asked this question, we did this work, we found this answer, it matters because." If it reads like a list, rewrite it.

Authorship & Credit

Ask your PI early: "What do I need to contribute to be a co-author?" In most fields, co-authors helped design the study, collected/analyzed data, or wrote sections. First author did the most work. Last author is often the PI/advisor. Middle authors contributed but less centrally. You want to be first or second author on your first paper. This shows leadership in that work.

LATAM Success Story: José M. Hernández

José Hernández was a farmworker's son from California who applied to NASA 11 times before being accepted as an astronaut. Before astronaut selection, he was an electrical engineer who did field research on equipment in challenging environments. He published findings on automation in farming. This research background, plus his persistence, made him competitive. He flew on STS-128 in 2009, then left NASA to write a memoir and star in the Netflix film "A Million Miles Away." His research wasn't glamorous, but it was rigorous, and it was his credentials.

LATAM Success Story: Katya Echazarreta

Katya Echazarreta was born in Mexico, grew up in California, and earned a degree in physics from Cal Tech. She did research at JPL on thermal systems and ion propulsion. That research led to papers and visibility. In 2022, she flew on Blue Origin's New Shepard rocket. Now she's a science communicator and consultant, still publishing on propulsion topics. Her papers and research were the foundation that let her transition to spaceflight and innovation. Research wasn't a stepping stone, it was her professional home.

Practice Exercise

Write Your Cold Email to a Real PI (60 minutes):

  1. Using Google Scholar, find a professor whose research interests you. Write down their name, institution, and one recent paper title.
  2. Read the abstract and introduction of that paper. Note one specific finding or method.
  3. Draft a cold email using the template. Make it personal to their work. Don't use the generic version.
  4. Have a friend or mentor review it. Ask: "Would this email make you take it seriously?"
  5. Wait one week. If you haven't sent it, send it now. You're not ready until you send.

Quiz: Research Opportunities & Paper Writing

Question 1: When writing a cold email to a potential research advisor, what should you include?
A) A vague interest in their field
B) Reference to one specific paper and a reason why you're interested in their work
C) Your entire CV and all past accomplishments
D) An apology for bothering them
Answer: B. Mentioning a specific recent paper shows you did homework. Vague interests get ignored. Long lists of credentials feel like spam.

Question 2: In the IMRaD paper structure, where should you explain what your results mean?
A) Introduction
B) Methods
C) Results
D) Discussion
Answer: D. Results just presents data. Discussion interprets findings, compares to prior research, and discusses implications.

Question 3: How many times should you expect to revise a paper draft with your advisor before it's ready for submission?
A) Once, first draft is final
B) 2-3 times
C) 3-5 times (or more)
D) Never; advisor writes it all
Answer: C. Research papers are revised many times. This is normal and how you learn to write clearly. Each round improves the work.

Question 4: What is the main advantage of presenting your research at a conference before submitting it to a journal?
A) It takes longer and is more prestigious
B) It's faster feedback, smaller peer-review community, and builds your visibility before a larger journal submission
C) You don't have to write a full paper
D) Conferences are the final step; you never submit to journals
Answer: B. Conferences are a good testing ground. Feedback is usually constructive. You meet other researchers in your field. Then you take that feedback and improve the work for journal submission.

Question 5: If a professor doesn't respond to your cold email, what should you do?
A) Give up; you're not qualified
B) Assume they're uninterested and move on to the next prospect
C) Email them repeatedly until they respond
D) Wait a week and send one polite follow-up; otherwise, move on
Answer: D. Non-response is normal (they're busy). Send one follow-up after a week. If still no response, contact another professor. Expect a 20 percent response rate on cold emails.

Writing papers is how your research becomes part of the permanent record. The first paper is the hardest, you'll doubt yourself, rewrite paragraphs ten times, and feel like an impostor. Then you hit submit. Six months later, it's published. Your name is there forever. That feeling, knowing you asked a question and found an answer that will help other scientists and engineers for years, that's why we do this work. You're closer to that than you think.
