Video Lesson: 10 Scripts for Speaking Success in Aerospace

Aracely demonstrates each script, shares pronunciation tips, and reveals what hiring managers actually listen for (approximately 10 minutes).

Video Script (for Aracely)

Today I want to give you ten scripts. These are not memorized speeches. These are frameworks. You memorize the structure and the key phrases, and then you make them yours. You adapt them to your personality and your story. A script that sounds like you is a hundred times more powerful than a script that sounds like a robot.

Script One is your elevator pitch. Thirty seconds. You are in an elevator with someone who works at SpaceX or NASA or Blue Origin. They ask, "What do you do?" You have thirty seconds to make them interested. Not thirty minutes. Thirty seconds.

You say: "Hi, I am [your name]. I am an engineer with [your specialty, for example, structural analysis or avionics systems] with [number] years of experience in [your industry or projects]. I am particularly interested in [your specific area: propulsion systems, thermal analysis, mission control]. Right now, I am focused on [what you are working on or learning]. I think the biggest opportunity in aerospace right now is [your insight: increased LATAM participation, reusable rocket technology, miniaturized satellites]. What do you work on?" Notice the last sentence. You ask a question back. You are not giving a monologue. You are starting a conversation.

Script Two is the technical interview opener. An interviewer asks, "Tell us about a technical project you led." You have two to three minutes. Here is the structure: Situation, Task, Action, Result. Situation: describe the project context in one sentence. Task: what was the challenge or the goal. Action: what did you do. Result: what was the outcome. Quantify when you can. "Improved thermal efficiency by twelve percent." Not "made it better." Numbers are proof.

Script Three is handling the difficult question: "What is your biggest weakness?" Do not say you have no weaknesses. Do not say you work too hard or care too much. That is not true. What you do is name a real challenge you faced and show how you addressed it. For example: "Early in my career, I struggled with the American style of direct communication in meetings. In Peru, we are taught to be indirect, to soften criticism. Here, people expect you to say what you think directly. I realized this was limiting my impact. I took a communication course. I practiced speaking up in meetings. Now I am comfortable saying, 'I disagree, and here is why.' That is still growing."

Script Four is the salary negotiation script. This one is sensitive. You are offered a position. You want more money. You do not say, "I need more money." You say: "I am thrilled about this opportunity. The role and the team are exactly what I am looking for. The salary is slightly below market rate for this level of experience and this specialty. Based on [research: Glassdoor, PayScale, your network], engineers with my background and [X years] experience in [your specialty] in [this region] typically earn between [lower number] and [higher number]. I would like to propose [your number], which reflects my experience and the value I bring. Is there flexibility in the budget?" This is not demanding. This is data-driven and respectful.

Script Five is the networking conversation at a conference. You see someone interesting. You walk over. You do not say, "Can I talk to you?" You say: "Hi, I heard your talk on [topic] earlier. I found your point about [specific detail] really interesting because [your reason]. I work in [your area], and we face a similar challenge. Have you found solutions that work across different applications?" Notice you are offering something: you acknowledge their expertise. Then you create a connection. Then you ask a question. You give them something to talk about, not yourself.

Script Six is asking for mentorship. You identify someone you admire. You write an email or you ask in person: "I have been following your work in [their field]. Your approach to [specific achievement] inspired me. I am working on [your field] and I would value your perspective on [specific question]. Would you be open to a fifteen-minute call in the next month to discuss [topic]?" Notice you are specific. You are not asking for hours of their time. You are asking for fifteen minutes. You show you have done your homework.

Script Seven is the technical presentation introduction. You are presenting a project or a design at a meeting. You start: "Good morning. My name is [name]. I am here to present our analysis of [topic]. I will cover three things: [first thing], [second thing], [third thing]. At the end, we will have time for questions. This work was done with [teams]. The motivation for this work was [the problem we are solving]. Let us start with [first point]." You are telling them what you will tell them, then you tell them, then you tell them what you told them. It is repetition. It helps people follow.

Script Eight is handling the "I do not know" moment. You are in an interview or a technical meeting. Someone asks you a question and you have no idea. What do you do? You do not panic. You do not make up an answer. You say: "That is a great question. I do not have that specific data in front of me right now. But here is how I would approach finding that answer. First, I would [logical step one]. Second, I would [logical step two]. If you are interested, I can research this and come back to you with the answer by [date]." You have shown your thinking process. You have shown you are reliable. You are not perfect. You are competent.

Script Nine is the follow-up email after a meeting or interview. You write: "Thank you for taking the time to meet with me today. I especially appreciated your insights on [specific thing they said]. I was particularly interested in the question about [their concern]. After our conversation, I realized [something you thought of]. I think [your insight] could help address that. I would like to [specific next step: send more information, schedule a follow-up]. I am still very interested in [the position or collaboration]. Please let me know if there is anything else you need from me." You are not desperate. You are professional. You are proactive.

Script Ten is the daily standup or update at work. You keep it to one minute. You say: "Yesterday I completed [task one] and [task two]. Today I am working on [task three]. The blocker I am facing is [issue], and I am addressing it by [your approach]. I am on track for [deadline]." Concise. Clear. Honest. If you have a blocker, you name it. Your manager wants to know about blockers early, not surprises later.

Now, about accent. I want to tell you about Franklin Chang-Díaz. He is from Costa Rica. He arrived in the US at eighteen years old. He spoke only Spanish. He earned an MIT PhD. He flew seven Space Shuttle missions. He leads major programs at NASA. His accent is absolutely still Costa Rican. If you listen to him on videos, he has an accent. No one cares. He is clear. He is confident. He is articulate. He has ideas. That is what matters.

Your accent is your heritage. It is where you come from. It is your Lambayeque, your Argentina, your Colombia. Do not erase it. Just make sure people can understand you. Speak clearly. Speak confidently. Speak slowly when you are presenting. Especially in the first five minutes when people are adjusting to your accent. Then you can go faster.

Here is your daily 30-minute routine to maintain speaking fluency for life:

Minutes 0-10: Shadowing. Pick a five to ten-minute aerospace YouTube video. Listen once without pausing. Listen again and try to copy the speaker's intonation and accent. You are not trying to perform. You are trying to absorb.

Minutes 10-20: Speaking practice. Use your iTalki tutor or a Tandem partner. Or practice alone. Pick a scenario from these ten scripts and speak it out loud. Record yourself. Speak for five minutes straight on a topic from your field. Do not worry about perfection. Focus on fluency. Can you speak for five minutes without stopping? That is the goal.

Minutes 20-30: Technical reading and reflection. Read one aerospace article or technical blog post. Read it out loud. Then write a three to four-sentence summary in your own words. This helps you practice comprehension and written expression at the same time.

This thirty-minute routine can be done at five in the morning before work. It can be done at lunch. It can be done on the train home. It does not require a teacher or a class. It requires commitment. Do this every single day and in two months you will not recognize your English.

The scripts I gave you are your toolkit. You do not memorize them word-for-word. You memorize them enough that when you are nervous or under pressure, your brain can access the structure. The structure keeps you from fumbling. The structure gives you confidence. And confidence, in English or in life, is everything.

10 Professional Scripts for Aerospace Success

Each script below is a framework. Memorize the structure. Personalize with your details. Practice out loud until it feels natural, not rehearsed.

Script 1: The 30-Second Elevator Pitch

Context: You meet a hiring manager or colleague at a conference or event. They ask, "What do you do?"

Framework:

"Hi, I'm [name]. I'm a [job title] with [number] years of experience in [industry/field]. My specialty is [specific area: structural analysis, avionics, mission control]. Right now I'm focused on [current project or learning]. I think the biggest opportunity in aerospace is [your insight]. What brings you to [event/company]?"

Key principle: End with a question. Turn the monologue into a conversation.

Script 2: The Technical Interview Project Story (STAR Method)

Context: Interviewer says, "Tell me about a technical project you led."

Framework (2-3 minutes):

Situation: "I led a thermal analysis project for [spacecraft/system] in [year]."
Task: "The challenge was [specific technical problem]. We needed to [outcome goal]."
Action: "I [your role]. I used [tools/methods]. I coordinated with [teams/people]. We tested [what you tested]. I documented [what you documented]."
Result: "We achieved [specific results with numbers]. The system performed [better/as designed]. The project outcome was [impact]."

Key principle: Use numbers. "Improved efficiency by fifteen percent" is stronger than "improved efficiency."

Script 3: Answering "What is Your Biggest Weakness?"

Context: Classic interview question meant to see if you are self-aware.

Framework:

"Early in my career, [describe real challenge: I struggled with direct communication, I had weak skills in X, I avoided public speaking]. I realized this was limiting my impact. I [took a course, found a mentor, practiced deliberately]. Now I [show growth]. I still work on this because [it is a lifetime skill], but I have concrete evidence it is better: [example of improvement]."

Key principle: Show self-awareness, action, and growth. Not perfection.

Script 4: Salary Negotiation

Context: You have been offered a position. The salary is below market. You want to negotiate.

Framework:

"I'm excited about this role and the team. This opportunity aligns with my career goals. The salary offered is slightly below market rate for [my level and specialty] in [location]. According to [Glassdoor, PayScale, your research], engineers with my background typically earn between [lower number] and [higher number]. I propose [your target number], which reflects my experience and market value. Is there flexibility in the budget? I'm also open to discussing other compensation like signing bonus, relocation, or flexible schedule."

Key principle: Data, respect, and flexibility. Negotiation is normal. Companies expect it.

Script 5: Networking at Conferences

Context: You see someone interesting at an aerospace conference. You approach them.

Framework:

"Hi, I attended your presentation on [topic]. Your point about [specific detail] really resonated with me because [your reason]. I work in [your area], and we face a similar challenge with [related problem]. Have you found solutions that scale across different applications? I'd love to learn from your experience."

Key principle: Compliment their work specifically. Show a connection. Ask a question that invites them to share expertise.

Script 6: Requesting Mentorship

Context: You want a mentor. You write an email or approach them at an event.

Framework:

"I've followed your work in [their specialty]. Your approach to [specific achievement or project] really inspired me. I'm working in [your field], and I'd value your perspective on [specific question]. Would you have time for a fifteen-minute call in [timeframe] to discuss [topic]? I'm happy to work around your schedule. Thank you for considering this."

Key principle: Specific, respectful of their time, and appreciative of their expertise. Not demanding.

Script 7: Technical Presentation Introduction

Context: You are presenting technical work in a meeting or conference.

Framework (first 2 minutes):

"Good morning. I'm [name], [title/role]. Today I'm presenting our [project type] on [topic]. I'll cover three areas: first, [area one] and why it matters; second, [area two] and our approach; third, [area three] and our results. This work was done in collaboration with [teams]. The motivation for this work is [the problem we're solving]. Let's start with [first point]."

Key principle: Tell them what you will tell them. Tell them. Tell them what you told them. Repetition helps comprehension.

Script 8: Handling "I Don't Know"

Context: In an interview, meeting, or technical discussion, someone asks a question you cannot answer.

Framework:

"That's a great question. I don't have that specific information in front of me right now. But here's how I'd approach finding the answer. First, I'd [logical step one]. Second, I'd [logical step two]. If you're interested, I can research this and get back to you by [specific date]. Does that work?"

Key principle: Show your thinking. Show you're reliable. Admitting what you don't know is stronger than guessing.

Script 9: Follow-Up Email After Interview or Meeting

Context: You have just interviewed or met with someone important. You send a follow-up email within 24 hours.

Framework:

"Thank you for taking the time to meet with me today. I especially appreciated your insights on [specific thing they shared]. After our conversation, I was thinking about [their concern or question]. I believe [your insight] could help address that. I'd like to [specific action: send research, schedule a follow-up, provide more information]. I remain very interested in [the opportunity/collaboration]. Please let me know if you need anything else from me. Best, [your name]"

Key principle: Timely, thoughtful, and proactive. You show you were listening and you are adding value.

Script 10: Daily Standup or Status Update

Context: At work, you give a daily or weekly update in a team meeting. Keep it to one minute.

Framework:

"Yesterday I completed [task one and task two]. Today I'm working on [task three]. The blocker I'm facing is [specific issue]. I'm addressing it by [your approach, timeline]. I'm on track for [milestone/deadline]. Any questions?"

Key principle: Concise, honest, and proactive about blockers. Good communication builds trust.

The 30-Minute Daily Speaking Routine

Practice this routine every single day for life. It maintains fluency. It does not require a teacher. It requires commitment.

Time Block Activity Goal How It Works
0-10 min Shadowing Practice Maintain accent recognition and listening comprehension Find one 5-10 min aerospace video. Listen once without pausing. Listen again and copy speaker intonation. Do NOT pause. Do NOT translate.
10-20 min Speaking Practice Maintain fluency and confidence in spontaneous speaking Choose a scenario from the 10 scripts above OR pick an aerospace topic you know well. Speak for 5-10 minutes straight, no pauses. Record yourself. Listen back for clarity, not perfection.
20-30 min Technical Reading and Reflection Maintain reading comprehension and written expression Read one aerospace article, blog post, or technical abstract out loud (pronunciation practice). Write a 3-4 sentence summary in your own words. This combines reading, speaking, and writing in one activity.

Schedule flexibility: This routine can be done in the morning before work, during lunch, on a commute, or in the evening. The key is consistency, not timing. Missing one day is okay. Missing a week breaks the habit. Stay committed to the habit.

Franklin Chang-Díaz: Accent as Asset, Not Liability

Franklin Chang-Díaz arrived in the United States from Costa Rica at age 18, speaking only Spanish. He earned a PhD from MIT in applied plasma physics. He flew 7 Space Shuttle missions (tied for the most spaceflights by any person). He founded Ad Astra Rocket Company and leads major propulsion research. His Costa Rican accent is unchanged. Here is what you should learn from his example:

  • Accent preservation: Your accent is not a deficiency. It is evidence of your international background. It makes you memorable. Hiring managers hear it and think, "Here is someone who crossed the world to be here."
  • Clarity over Americanization: Chang-Díaz does not try to sound American. He speaks clearly. He speaks confidently. He speaks at a measured pace. That is what matters. You are not trying to hide where you come from. You are trying to communicate.
  • Expertise overcomes everything: Chang-Díaz is listened to because he is an expert in plasma propulsion. If you are an expert, your accent becomes irrelevant. The content is what matters.
  • Representation matters: Every time a Latino aerospace professional speaks with confidence and an accent, it opens the door for the next person. You are not just building your career. You are building a path for others.

Knowledge Check Quiz

Q1: The STAR method for technical interviews stands for?

A) Smart, Technical, Analytical, Results
B) Situation, Task, Action, Result
C) Specific, Time-bound, Achievement, Reflection
D) Systems, Testing, Analysis, Reasoning

Answer: B. Situation (context), Task (goal), Action (what you did), Result (outcome with numbers when possible).

Q2: How long is the 30-minute daily speaking routine?

A) 15 minutes
B) 30 minutes
C) 1 hour
D) 2 hours

Answer: B. 30 minutes total: 10 min shadowing, 10 min speaking practice, 10 min technical reading. Daily consistency matters more than long sessions.

Q3: What is Franklin Chang-Díaz an example of?

A) How to lose your accent and become American
B) How accent does not prevent aerospace success if you are clear and expert
C) That you need an American accent to fly space shuttles
D) That Latinos cannot reach leadership in aerospace

Answer: B. Chang-Díaz arrived speaking only Spanish, earned MIT PhD, flew 7 space shuttle missions, founded rocket company, leads major research. His Costa Rican accent is unchanged. He proves expertise and clarity override accent.
