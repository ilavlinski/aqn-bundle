Module 5: University and Admissions

Lesson Objectives

  • Understand full-cost scholarships and need-based aid available to Latin American students
  • Learn the difference between university aid, government scholarships, and private foundations

  • Master the timeline and strategy for scholarship applications
  • Explore graduate funding models: teaching assistantships, research assistantships, and fellowships

The Scholarship Landscape for Latin American STEM Students

When I was deciding where to study, I realized something crucial: cost cannot be the barrier. It cannot be. The US has built pathways to fund talented students regardless of financial background. Those pathways exist. You need to find them, apply strategically, and persist.

I earned seven degrees. I paid for them through a combination of scholarships, assistantships, and strategic financial planning. No single degree was funded the same way. I want to map out what is available to you.

Three funding categories exist: (1) Full-cost university aid (need-blind or merit-based), (2) Government and bilateral scholarships (country-specific programs), and (3) Private foundations and international organizations.

University Aid: Need-Blind Admissions for International Students

Myth: International students cannot get financial aid at US universities.

Reality: A small number of elite universities offer need-blind admission to international undergraduates. This means they admit you based on academic merit without considering your ability to pay, then fund your entire education.

Universities with need-blind international aid: MIT, Stanford, Harvard, Princeton, Caltech, Yale, Dartmouth, Williams, Amherst, Bowdoin.

Cost after aid at these schools: A family earning 65,000 dollars per year pays 0 dollars tuition. A family earning 150,000 dollars per year pays about 10,000 dollars per year. Full-pay families still pay full tuition, but the bottom 60 to 70% of income levels pay proportionally less.

For graduate students: Almost all R1 universities provide full funding (tuition plus 25,000 to 45,000 dollars per year salary) through teaching assistantships (TA) and research assistantships (RA) for PhD students. Master's students have less guaranteed funding but can often find TA/RA positions after admission.

Cost Projection: 4-Year Undergraduate Education

MIT/Stanford/Harvard (private elite with full aid): With need-blind aid, your family contribution is proportional to income. Average international student with low-income background: 0 to 5,000 dollars per year out-of-pocket.

Purdue/Georgia Tech/UIUC (public universities, no need-blind international aid): Out-of-state tuition: 30,000 to 33,000 dollars per year. Room and board: 13,000 to 15,000 dollars per year. Books and supplies: 1,200 dollars per year. Total: approximately 45,000 dollars per year, or 180,000 dollars for 4 years.

University scholarships for public schools: Some states (Texas, Ohio, Florida) offer merit scholarships for top international students (10,000 to 15,000 dollars per year).

Combined strategy: Full Fulbright or Mastercard + public university = 0 out-of-pocket cost. Merit scholarship (10,000 dollars) + co-op salary (12,000 to 18,000 dollars per year) + public university = possibly net positive cash flow.

Graduate Funding Model: The TA/RA Assistantship

This is the game-changer for graduate students. Nearly all R1 universities fund PhD students and many Master's students through assistantships.

Teaching Assistantship (TA): You lead lab sections, grade assignments, and conduct office hours for undergraduate courses. Typical time commitment: 10 to 20 hours per week. Typical pay: 800 to 1,400 dollars per month. University pays full tuition (30,000 to 65,000 dollars per year). Health insurance included.

Research Assistantship (RA): You work directly on a faculty member's research project (often NASA-funded or aerospace industry). Typical time commitment: 20 hours per week. Typical pay: 1,000 to 2,000 dollars per month. University pays full tuition. Health insurance included.

Fellowship: Competitive grants from universities, NASA, NSF, or private foundations. No teaching or assistantship work required. You focus exclusively on research. Typical pay: 20,000 to 30,000 dollars per year plus tuition. Highly competitive (5 to 10% acceptance rate).

Real numbers for an international Master's student: At Purdue or Georgia Tech, an RA position covers tuition (15,000 to 18,000 dollars per year for Master's) plus 1,000 to 1,400 dollars monthly salary. Out-of-pocket cost: zero. You may even save money if living costs are low.

Video Lesson: How I Funded Seven Degrees (10 minutes)

Full Video Script (Aracely speaking, 950 words)

When people ask me how I earned seven degrees, the first question they ask after is always the same: How did you pay for them? That is a fair question. Let me be direct: I did not have wealthy parents who could write checks. I did not have a safety net. I had to be strategic.

Let me walk you through how I funded my education, because the model I used is available to you too.

First degree. I applied for scholarships before I did anything else. I looked for government programs in Peru that funded students to study abroad. I looked for international organizations. Fulbright was one. But I also looked locally. Peru has bilateral agreements with other countries. I explored those. It took time. It took paperwork. It took persistence. But I found funding that covered my undergraduate degree. Not all of it, but enough that I could work part-time and cover the rest.

Part-time work in the US is legal for international students on an F-1 visa, but limited to 20 hours per week on campus. That is not enough to cover full tuition. But it covers room and board. It covers books. So my scholarship covered tuition, and my part-time job covered living costs. That was my model for the first degree.

Second and third degrees: Master's programs. Here is where the model changed completely. When I applied to Master's programs, I did not look only for full scholarships. I looked for assistantship positions. Teaching assistantships. Research assistantships. I asked faculty members directly: do you have funding for a research assistant? If they said yes, I knew my tuition would be covered. I knew I would be paid a salary. Even though the salary was modest, it was enough to live on.

I worked as a research assistant on a spacecraft propulsion project. The professor had a grant from the National Science Foundation. That grant paid my tuition. It paid my salary. It paid for me to attend conferences and present my research. For two years, I was fully funded. And I was earning experience I could put on my resume.

This is not luck. This is strategy. When you apply to a Master's program, you are not just applying to the university. You are applying to work for a specific faculty member. You need to find faculty whose research interests match yours. You need to email them. You need to say: I am interested in working on your project. What positions do you have available? Some will say no. Some will say yes. If one or two say yes, you can negotiate with the university to guarantee your funding.

Fourth through seventh degrees: PhD programs and advanced specializations. By this point, I was experienced. I knew what I was looking for. I looked at universities where NASA was actively recruiting. I looked at research areas that matched NASA's mission priorities. Then I found faculty members working on those specific projects. Many were funded by NASA directly. When I approached them about applying to their PhD program, they already had funding secured. I did not go to the university first. I went to the professor first.

A PhD is typically a five to seven year commitment. During that time, you are paid. You work as a teaching assistant or research assistant. Most students work 15 to 20 hours per week while doing their own research. Your advisor funds your tuition. Your salary is between 1,500 and 2,500 dollars per month, depending on your seniority and the field. After five years, you have a degree, a salary saved, and years of research experience. The university is investing in you because your research contributes to their mission.

Here is what I wish I had known earlier: the scholarship landscape changes completely if you understand the difference between merit scholarships (which are competitive and limited) and assistantships (which are abundant and tied to research funding). Most international students focus on merit scholarships. They are the wrong target. Focus instead on finding a professor with a funded research project that interests you. That professor can pay for your education.

I also want to name something specific: the Fulbright program. If you are a citizen of a Latin American country, your country likely has a Fulbright program. Peru's Fulbright program is strong. Colombia's is strong. Mexico's is very strong. These programs cover 100% of tuition plus monthly stipend. The competition is real, but it is less intense than applying to Harvard. If you want a master's degree from a top US university, Fulbright is often the most realistic path.

The same applies to the Mastercard Foundation Scholars program. If you come from a family earning less than 25,000 dollars per year, you are eligible. Full tuition. Room and board. Books. All covered. Four years. The program has placed thousands of African students at top universities. The same opportunity exists for Latin Americans.

Here is the strategy I recommend for you, in order:

First, check if your country has a government scholarship program. Most do. Brazil, Colombia, Mexico, Peru, Chile all have them. Apply there first. The competition is lower. The funding is substantial.

Second, if you are applying to a Master's program, do not just apply to the university. Email ten professors whose research matches your interests. Tell them you are interested in their research. Ask if they have funding for a graduate student. You will likely get rejected by eight. Two might say yes. If one says yes, you have your path forward.

Third, if you are applying as an undergraduate, focus on universities with need-blind admission for international students. MIT, Stanford, Harvard, Princeton. Yes, they are selective. Yes, the acceptance rate is low. But if you are admitted, you will be fully funded. Your chance of admission is higher if you apply strategically. I recommend targeting aerospace programs. They have smaller applicant pools than business or finance. Your relative strength in aerospace might be higher.

Fourth, learn the visa rules. An F-1 visa allows 20 hours per week of on-campus work. That is 80 hours per month. At 15 dollars per hour, that is 1,200 dollars per month. Over a year, that is 14,400 dollars. That is not enough to pay for everything, but it is meaningful.

Finally, remember that funding is not a one-time decision. You can piece it together. Scholarship for one year. Assistantship for two years. Work-study for another year. Different degrees. Different strategies. Different funding sources. You are not looking for one perfect scholarship that solves everything. You are building a mosaic.

Seven degrees was possible because I was willing to be strategic about funding. I do not regret a single one. Each degree opened a door I could not have seen before.

Your path will not look like mine. But the doors are open. Go find them.

Knowledge Check Quiz

Q1: Which of the following universities offer need-blind admission to international undergraduate students?

A) MIT, Stanford, Harvard, Princeton, Caltech
B) Purdue, Georgia Tech, UIUC, University of Texas
C) Only Ivy League universities
D) No US universities offer this

Answer: A (Explanation: A small group of elite universities (approximately 10 globally) offer need-blind admission to international students. MIT, Stanford, Harvard, Princeton, and Caltech are among them. They admit based on merit and then fund your entire education.)

Q2: What is the typical monthly salary for a graduate research assistant at an R1 university?

A) 300 to 500 dollars
B) 800 to 1,400 dollars
C) 2,500 to 4,000 dollars
D) 5,000 to 8,000 dollars

Answer: B (Explanation: Graduate research assistants at R1 universities typically earn 800 to 1,400 dollars per month (or approximately 12,000 to 18,000 dollars per year) while their university pays full tuition and health insurance.)

Q3: Which scholarship program covers 100% of costs for low-income international students from any country?

A) Fulbright
B) Mastercard Foundation Scholars
C) Chevening
D) DAAD

Answer: B (Explanation: The Mastercard Foundation Scholars program provides 100% tuition, room, board, and books for students from low-income families (typically under 25,000 dollars annual family income) from any country.)

Q4: What is the strategy for securing a Master's program assistantship?

A) Apply to the university and wait for their funding offer
B) Identify faculty members with funded research projects, email them directly, and negotiate funding before formally applying to the university
C) Get admitted first, then ask the department for funding
D) Only available to domestic students

Answer: B (Explanation: The most effective strategy is to find faculty members whose research interests match yours, email them directly asking about funding, and negotiate assistantship funding before submitting your formal application to the university. This flips the traditional order and increases your chance of funding.)

Q5: For a 4-year undergraduate degree at a public R1 university without aid, what is the approximate total cost?

A) 45,000 dollars
B) 90,000 dollars
C) 180,000 dollars
D) 240,000 dollars

Answer: C (Explanation: At a public R1 university (Purdue, Georgia Tech, UIUC), out-of-state tuition is approximately 30,000 to 33,000 dollars per year, plus room and board at 13,000 to 15,000 dollars per year. Total per year: approximately 45,000 dollars. Four years: approximately 180,000 dollars without aid.)
