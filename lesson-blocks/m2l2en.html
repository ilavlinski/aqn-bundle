Video Lesson: 100 Aerospace Terms You Must Know

Aracely breaks down essential vocabulary, pronunciation, and real-world usage in aerospace engineering (approximately 9 minutes).

Video Script (for Aracely)

When I first sat down to work on TRMM, the Tropical Rainfall Measuring Mission, I opened the technical specifications. I understood maybe thirty percent. Why? Because I knew English. I did not know aerospace English. There is a difference.

Technical vocabulary is not just bigger words. It is a language within the language. Aerospace has its own grammar, its own collocations, its own way of saying things. You cannot just translate from Spanish word-for-word. It does not work.

For example, in Spanish you might say "presión del combustible." In aerospace English, we say "propellant pressure." In Spanish, "temperatura del motor." In aerospace, "engine nozzle temperature." The words matter. The order matters. The collocation matters.

There are one hundred terms you absolutely must know. Not to pass a test. To do your job. To understand emails from colleagues. To read specifications. To present technical work. To survive in a meeting where five native speakers are throwing around aerospace jargon at conversation speed.

These terms fall into seven categories. Propulsion. Avionics. Thermodynamics. Orbital mechanics. Guidance and navigation. Aerodynamics. Structural analysis and materials science.

Let me start with propulsion because it is where most of your career will start. A rocket engine burns fuel. The fuel is called propellant. There is liquid propellant and solid propellant. Liquid propellant is stored in tanks called tanks. Solid propellant comes as blocks called propellant grains. The burn rate of the propellant grain determines how long the engine burns. This is called burn duration. The amount of propellant consumed per unit of time is called the burn rate.

The engine produces thrust. Thrust is measured in pounds-force or kilonewtons. Specific impulse, abbreviated Isp, measures how efficiently the engine uses propellant. Higher Isp means more efficient engine. Isp is measured in seconds. A good rocket engine might have Isp of three hundred seconds. That is a vocabulary phrase: "three hundred second Isp." Not "three hundred seconds of specific impulse." Say "Isp." Everyone knows what that means.

The nozzle is the part of the engine that directs the hot gases. The nozzle throat is the narrowest point. Gases accelerate through the nozzle and exit at very high velocity. The nozzle exit velocity is another crucial term.

In avionics, the field gets even more specialized. Avionics refers to all the electronic systems on a spacecraft or aircraft. An avionics suite is a package of integrated systems. There is guidance and navigation, abbreviated GN ampersand C. Guidance is which direction the vehicle goes. Navigation is where the vehicle is. Control is how to steer it.

The inertial measurement unit, or IMU, measures acceleration. Accelerometers inside the IMU detect motion. Gyroscopes detect rotation. Together, they tell the spacecraft where it is going.

For communications, there is the antenna. A transmitter sends signals. A receiver picks them up. The signal frequency is measured in gigahertz. Signal strength is measured in decibels.

In thermodynamics, you are dealing with heat. Thermal analysis studies how heat moves through the spacecraft. Radiative heat transfer is heat that moves through radiation. Conductive heat transfer is heat that moves through direct contact. Convective heat transfer is heat moved by moving fluid or gas.

Thermal protection systems, or TPS, are the materials that protect the spacecraft from extreme heat. During reentry, the outside of the spacecraft reaches temperatures over three thousand Kelvin. That is hot enough to melt most metals. The TPS material ablates, meaning it slowly burns away and carries heat away from the spacecraft.

In orbital mechanics, you are dealing with orbits. A circular orbit is an orbit where the altitude stays the same. An elliptical orbit is an orbit where the altitude changes. The point closest to Earth is called the perigee. The point farthest from Earth is called the apogee. The height of the orbit is called the altitude.

Orbital velocity is the speed needed to maintain orbit without falling to Earth. At lower altitudes, orbital velocity is faster. At higher altitudes, orbital velocity is slower. For the International Space Station at about four hundred kilometers altitude, orbital velocity is about seven point seven kilometers per second.

A geosynchronous orbit is an orbit where the spacecraft stays above the same point on Earth. The orbital period equals Earth's rotation period, which is twenty-four hours. Many communication satellites are in geosynchronous orbit.

In aerodynamics, you are dealing with air. The fuselage is the main body of the aircraft. The wings generate lift. Drag is air resistance. The center of pressure is the point where all aerodynamic forces act. The center of gravity is where the weight is concentrated. If the center of pressure is behind the center of gravity, the aircraft is unstable. If it is ahead, the aircraft is stable.

Mach number is the speed relative to the speed of sound. Mach 1 is the speed of sound. Mach 2 is twice the speed of sound. In thick air near sea level, the speed of sound is about 340 meters per second. At high altitude where air is thin, sound does not travel far.

Structural analysis is about materials and stress. Stress is force divided by area. Strain is how much a material deforms under stress. The yield strength is the stress at which the material starts to permanently deform. The ultimate strength is the stress at which the material breaks.

Young's modulus is a measure of how stiff a material is. Density is mass per unit volume. Titanium is used in aerospace because it has low density and high strength. We say titanium has a high strength-to-weight ratio.

Composite materials are made of two materials mixed together. Carbon fiber composite is carbon fibers embedded in a resin. Composite materials are lighter than metals and nearly as strong.

Now, the mistake that Spanish speakers make. You see words like "presión" and "presion" and think they are the same. They are not. "Presión" in Spanish is "pressure" in English. That is a cognate. That part is fine. But then you hear "pressure vessel" and you think it means "vasija de presión." No. A pressure vessel is a container that holds high-pressure fluid. In Spanish aerospace, it might be called a "tanque de presión" or "recipiente a presión." You need to memorize which English words go together.

Another mistake. You learn the word "embarazada" which sounds like "embarrassed." They are not the same. "Embarazada" means pregnant. "Embarrassed" means feeling ashamed or self-conscious. If you say you are embarrassed to present your work, no one will think you are pregnant. But you need to know the difference.

Or "constipated." In Spanish it is "constipado," which means you have a cold. In English, "constipated" means your digestive system is not working. These false cognates will trip you up if you do not study them.

The TOEFL exam tests whether you know general English. It does not test aerospace vocabulary. But the IELTS Academic exam, especially in the writing and speaking sections, can include technical content. You need aerospace vocabulary for your actual job, not just for the exam.

TOEFL has four sections. Reading, listening, speaking, writing. Each section is scored from zero to thirty. Total score is zero to one hundred twenty. The reading section has three to four passages of about seven hundred words each. You have sixty minutes to read all passages and answer questions. The questions test whether you understand main ideas, details, inference, and vocabulary.

The listening section has three to four lectures and two to three conversations. Each lecture is about five minutes. Each conversation is about three minutes. You listen once. You cannot replay. You take notes and answer questions. The listening section is eighteen to twenty minutes of audio with about twelve to sixteen minutes of time to answer questions.

The speaking section has four tasks. Two independent speaking tasks where you speak about yourself and your opinions. Two integrated speaking tasks where you listen to audio and then speak about what you heard. Each response is about sixty seconds. You speak into a microphone and your response is recorded.

The writing section has two tasks. One integrated writing task where you read a passage, listen to a lecture, and write about the relationship. The response should be one hundred fifty to two hundred twenty-five words. One independent writing task where you write an essay about your opinion on a topic. The response should be three hundred words or more. You have fifty minutes total for both writing tasks.

IELTS is organized differently. It has listening, reading, writing, and speaking. Listening is forty minutes with ten recorded audio sections. Reading is sixty minutes with three sections and about forty questions total. Writing is sixty minutes with two tasks, one nine-hundred-character task and one four-hundred-word essay. Speaking is face-to-face with a test examiner. The speaking test is eleven to fourteen minutes divided into three parts.

For aerospace engineers, IELTS might feel slightly more familiar because the speaking is conversational rather than recorded. But TOEFL is more common in the United States. If you are applying to NASA or SpaceX or going to graduate school at MIT or Stanford, TOEFL is the safer choice.

The secret to scoring high is not being perfect. It is being consistent. In reading, you need to understand seventy to eighty percent of the passage to answer the questions correctly. In listening, you need to understand seventy to eighty percent of what you hear. In speaking, you need to be clear and organized, not perfect. Native speakers are not perfect in the TOEFL exam either.

Start your vocabulary sprint now. You have six weeks. Week one, learn terms one through fifteen in all seven categories. Week two, terms sixteen through thirty. By week six, you have learned all one hundred terms and you have seen each one multiple times in Anki through spaced repetition. That is how you remember. Not by cramming. By consistent review over time.

Build flashcards. On the front, the English term with pronunciation. On the back, the Spanish equivalent, a one-line definition, and an example sentence from a real aerospace document. Review every day. Your future self will thank you.

Orbital Circular Orbit Órbita Circular An orbit where altitude remains constant "The ISS maintains a circular orbit."
Orbital Elliptical Orbit Órbita Elíptica An orbit where altitude varies, with perigee and apogee "The transfer orbit is elliptical."

Aerodynamics (15 terms)
Aero Fuselage Fuselaje The main body of an aircraft or launch vehicle "The fuselage houses fuel, payload, and avionics."
Aero Wing Ala The aerodynamic surface that generates lift "Wing design determines aircraft efficiency."
Aero Lift Sustentación The upward aerodynamic force perpendicular to airflow "Lift balances weight in steady flight."
Aero Drag Resistencia Aerodinámica The aerodynamic force opposing motion through air "Minimizing drag improves fuel efficiency."
Aero Center of Pressure Centro de Presión The point where all aerodynamic forces act "The center of pressure moves with speed and altitude."
Aero Center of Gravity Centro de Gravedad The point where all weight is concentrated "Center of gravity must remain forward of center of pressure for stability."
Aero Aerodynamic Stability Estabilidad Aerodinámica The vehicle's natural tendency to maintain stable flight path "Aerodynamic stability is critical for launch vehicle control."
Aero Mach Number Número de Mach Speed relative to local speed of sound (Mach 1 is sonic speed) "The aircraft climbs to Mach 2."
Aero Speed of Sound Velocidad del Sonido The speed at which sound waves propagate through air, approximately 340 m/s at sea level "Speed of sound decreases at higher altitude."
Aero Supersonic Supersónico Flying faster than the speed of sound (Mach 1 or greater) "Supersonic flight generates shock waves."
Aero Transonic Transónico Flying near the speed of sound, typically Mach 0.8 to 1.2 "Transonic flow creates complex shock wave patterns."
Aero Hypersonic Hipersónico Flying at very high supersonic speeds, typically Mach 5 or greater "Hypersonic reentry requires special thermal protection."
Aero Boundary Layer Capa Límite The thin layer of fluid near a surface where viscous effects are dominant "Boundary layer separation causes flow instability."
Aero Stall Pérdida A loss of lift when airflow separates from a wing at high angle of attack "The aircraft must avoid stalling during climb."

Struct Stress Estrés Internal force per unit area (Force divided by Area) "Material stress exceeds yield strength."
Struct Strain Deformación Relative deformation of a material under stress (Change in length divided by original length) "Strain measurements indicate material damage."
Struct Yield Strength Límite Elástico The stress level at which a material begins permanent deformation "Aluminum yield strength is limited by design stress."
Struct Ultimate Strength Resistencia Última The maximum stress a material can withstand before breaking "Ultimate strength determines structural safety margins."
Struct Young's Modulus Módulo de Young A measure of material stiffness (stress divided by strain in the elastic region) "Titanium has a high Young's modulus."
Struct Density Densidad Mass per unit volume of a material "Aluminum density is three grams per cubic centimeter."
Struct Strength-to-Weight Ratio Relación Resistencia Peso Material strength divided by material density; higher is better for aerospace "Titanium has an excellent strength-to-weight ratio."
Struct Fatigue Fatiga Progressive material failure under repeated loading cycles "Fatigue analysis is critical for rocket motor cases."
Struct Fracture Mechanics Mecánica de Fractura The study of crack growth and material failure under stress "Fracture mechanics predicts structural failure."
Struct Composite Material Material Compuesto A material made from two or more constituent materials with different properties "Carbon fiber composites are used throughout the spacecraft."
Struct Carbon Fiber Fibra de Carbono Strong, lightweight fibers made of carbon, typically embedded in resin "Carbon fiber reduces structural mass significantly."
Struct Titanium Alloy Aleación de Titanio A metal alloy based on titanium, combining strength and lightness "Titanium alloys withstand high temperatures."
Struct Aluminum Alloy Aleación de Aluminio A metal alloy based on aluminum, lightweight but less strong than titanium "Aluminum alloys are used for non-critical structures."
Struct Buckling Pandeo Sudden lateral instability of a structural member under compression "Column buckling analysis prevents structural collapse."
Struct Safety Factor Factor de Seguridad The ratio of material strength to applied stress; higher is safer "We design with a safety factor of 1.5 for primary structures."
Struct Vibration Analysis Análisis de Vibración Study of mechanical oscillations in structures "Vibration analysis predicts resonance frequencies."

Systems Subsystem Subsistema A major component or system that contributes to overall spacecraft function "Each subsystem has redundant components."
Systems Interface Interfaz The connection or boundary between two systems or components "The electrical interface specifies voltage and current requirements."
Systems Integration Integración The process of combining subsystems into a complete spacecraft "System integration testing verifies all subsystem interactions."
Systems Margin Margen The difference between predicted performance and actual requirement, providing safety buffer "Power margin ensures adequate system reserves."
Systems Design Margin Margen de Diseño Built-in performance buffer in design beyond minimum requirement "Ten percent design margin is standard."
Systems Test Margin Margen de Prueba Performance buffer shown during ground testing beyond flight requirements "Test margin demonstrates design adequacy."
Systems Specification Especificación A detailed technical document defining requirements for a system or component "The specification defines maximum operating temperature."
Systems Requirements Requisitos The formal statements of what a system must do or how it must perform "Mission requirements drive all design decisions."
Systems Performance Rendimiento How well a system accomplishes its intended function "System performance exceeds specification."
Systems Reliability Confiabilidad The probability that a system operates without failure for a specified duration "Reliability exceeds ninety-nine point nine percent."
Systems Failure Mode Modo de Fallo The specific way a component or system can fail "Critical failure modes are eliminated by design."
Systems Failure Mode and Effects Analysis (FMEA) Análisis de Modo de Fallo y Efectos A systematic method for identifying potential failures and their consequences "FMEA identified critical thermal protection gaps."
Systems Load Path Ruta de Carga The structural path through which forces are transmitted "Proper load path design ensures structural integrity."
Systems Mass Budget Presupuesto de Masa The allocation of total spacecraft mass to subsystems "Mass budget constraints drive lightweight design."
Systems Power Budget Presupuesto de Potencia The allocation of total electrical power to subsystems "Power budget allocation limits transmitter duty cycle."
Systems Thermal Budget Presupuesto Térmico The allocation of total heat dissipation capability to subsystems "Thermal budget determines radiator size."
Systems Mission Operations Operaciones de Misión The real-time command and monitoring of spacecraft during its mission "Mission operations centers manage spacecraft 24/7."

False Cognates and Common Mistakes for Spanish Speakers

Spanish Word Meaning in Spanish English False Cognate Actual English Meaning Correct English in Aerospace
Constipado/a Having a cold or runny nose Constipated Unable to have bowel movements Use "have a cold" or "have a runny nose"
Embarazada Pregnant Embarrassed Feeling ashamed or self-conscious "I was embarrassed to present." Use for shame, not pregnancy.
Actualmente Currently, at the present time Actually In fact, really (contradicting expectation) "Currently we are testing" not "Actually we are testing" in formal contexts
Éxito Success Suceso An event or occurrence "The mission was a success." Use "success" for achievement.
Realizar To carry out, accomplish, perform Realize To become aware of, understand "We performed a test" not "We realized a test." "We realized the problem" means we became aware of it.
Sensible Sensitive, perceptive Sensible Reasonable, practical, showing good sense "A sensible approach" = reasonable approach. "A sensitive sensor" = a perceptive sensor.
Soportar To tolerate, endure, bear Support To help, hold up, or provide backing "Support the structure" (hold it up). "Tolerate high temperature" (bear it).
Aplicación Application, use Application Also application, but in aerospace refers to uses of technology Usually cognates correctly. "Aerospace applications" = spacecraft uses of technology.
Fábrica Factory Fabric Cloth or material woven from fibers "Made at the factory." Avoid "fabricated" when you mean "manufactured."
Introducción Introduction Introduction Also introduction, but sometimes in Spanish "introducción" can mean "insertion" "Introduction to the topic" (standard). "Insertion of fuel" (less common in aerospace).

TOEFL iBT Structure and Scoring

Total Score Range: 0-120 points

Test Duration: Approximately 3 hours (including breaks)

Section breakdown:

  • Reading: 3-4 passages, 10-14 questions per passage. Total 60 minutes. Score range: 0-30.
  • Listening: 3-4 lectures, 2-3 conversations. Total 60-90 minutes of audio with questions. Score range: 0-30.
  • Speaking: 4 tasks (2 independent, 2 integrated). 20 minutes total. Responses recorded. Score range: 0-30.
  • Writing: 2 tasks (1 integrated, 1 independent). 50 minutes total. Score range: 0-30.

Scoring interpretation:

  • 120: Perfect score (rare)
  • 100-120: Strong candidate for top universities and NASA
  • 80-99: Acceptable for most US universities and employers
  • 60-79: Basic English competency, may need support
  • Below 60: Significant gaps in English proficiency

IELTS Academic Structure and Scoring

Total Score Range: 1-9 bands (0.5 band increments)

Test Duration: Approximately 2 hours 45 minutes

Section breakdown:

  • Listening: 10 recorded sections, 40 questions. 30 minutes plus 10 minutes transfer time. Score: 0-40 raw, converted to 1-9 band.
  • Reading (Academic): 3 passages, 40 questions. 60 minutes. Score: 0-40 raw, converted to 1-9 band.
  • Writing: 2 tasks (150-word description, 250-word essay). 60 minutes. Score: 1-9 band based on task achievement, coherence, lexical range, grammatical accuracy.
  • Speaking: 3 parts face-to-face with examiner. 11-14 minutes. Part 1 is introduction and familiar topics (4-5 min). Part 2 is a prepared topic (3-4 min). Part 3 is discussion (4-5 min). Score: 1-9 band.

Band score interpretation:

  • 8.5-9.0: Expert user. Fully operational command. Makes rare errors.
  • 7.5-8.0: Very good user. Operational command. Occasional errors or inappropriate usage.
  • 7.0: Good user. Operational command. Makes errors in unfamiliar situations. Generally maintains control.
  • 6.5: Competent user. Generally effective command. Occasional errors and inappropriate usage.
  • 6.0: Acceptable user. Generally effective command in familiar situations. Makes frequent errors.
  • Below 6.0: Limited proficiency for academic study or professional work.

Knowledge Check Quiz

Q1: What does "specific impulse" (Isp) measure?

A) The speed of the rocket
B) How efficiently the engine uses propellant
C) The temperature inside the combustion chamber
D) The weight of the payload

Answer: B. Higher Isp means more efficient propellant usage. A typical high-performance engine has Isp around 300-450 seconds.

Q2: The point in an elliptical orbit closest to Earth is called?

A) Apogee
B) Perigee
C) Altitude
D) Velocity point

Answer: B. Perigee is closest (think "peri" = near). Apogee is farthest (think "apo" = away from).

Q3: What is a "thermal protection system" (TPS)?

A) The heating system inside the spacecraft
B) Materials that shield spacecraft from extreme heat during reentry
C) Radiators that cool the spacecraft
D) Insulation between fuel tanks

Answer: B. TPS materials ablate (burn away slowly) during reentry, carrying heat away from the spacecraft structure.

Q4: Which is a false cognate that trips up Spanish speakers?

A) Comunicación (communication)
B) Electricidad (electricity)
C) Actualmente (actually)
D) Tecnología (technology)

Answer: C. "Actualmente" in Spanish means "currently" or "at present." "Actually" in English means "in fact" or "really," contradicting expectation. These are not the same.

Q5: What is the total score range for TOEFL iBT?

A) 0-100
B) 0-120
C) 1-9
D) 0-90

Answer: B. TOEFL iBT total score ranges from 0-120 (four sections of 0-30 each). IELTS uses 1-9 band scale.

Q6: How long is the speaking section of TOEFL iBT?

A) 5 minutes
B) 10 minutes
C) 20 minutes
D) 30 minutes

Answer: C. 20 minutes total. Four tasks, with independent and integrated speaking. Each response is about 60 seconds recorded into a microphone.
