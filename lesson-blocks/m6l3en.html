My Path to Permanent Residence

I came through an extraordinary ability pathway, which is rare. But I want to walk you through the more common pathways because most aerospace engineers you know will use them. Green card sponsorship is a process, not magic. It takes 2-5 years after your employer or you initiate it. It costs money. It requires an attorney. But at the end, you have permanent residence, and that changes everything.

Permanent residence means: you can change jobs freely, you can start your own company, you can travel in and out of the US without worrying about visa expiration, you can sponsor family members to immigrate, and you are on the path to citizenship (5 more years). This is the goal. This is what we are working toward.

Employment-Based Green Card Categories

There are four primary employment-based green card categories (EB-1, EB-2, EB-3, EB-4). Aerospace engineers typically use EB-1A, EB-2 NIW, or EB-2 PERM.

EB-1A (Extraordinary Ability): Green card for individuals with extraordinary ability in their field. This is the rarest and most prestigious. You must demonstrate extraordinary ability at a level above O-1A. Many academics, researchers, and some high-profile engineers qualify. Example: a researcher with multiple patents, international recognition, and significant publications.

EB-1B (Outstanding Professor or Researcher): Green card for outstanding professors or researchers with at least 3 years of experience. Typically used for academics at universities or senior researchers at labs.

EB-2 NIW (National Interest Waiver): Green card for professionals with advanced degree (master's or PhD) whose work is in the national interest. This is the most common pathway for aerospace engineers with advanced degrees. Unlike EB-2 PERM, you do NOT need an employer to petition for you. You can self-petition. The "national interest" includes space exploration, defense technology, climate research, and many other fields where US interests are served. The test is called "Matter of Dhanasar" and has three prongs (see below).

EB-2 PERM (Permanent Labor Certification): Green card for professionals with bachelor's degree or advanced degree, sponsored by an employer. Your employer must show they tried to recruit US workers first and could not find qualified candidates. This is labor certification. It is lengthy (1-3 years) but very common. Most H-1B workers eventually pursue EB-2 PERM with their employer.

EB-3 (Skilled Worker): Green card for skilled workers with bachelor's degree. Similar to EB-2 PERM but with lower priority. Less common for aerospace professionals.

EB-2 NIW: Matter of Dhanasar 3-Prong Test

Matter of Dhanasar is a 2016 USCIS precedent that defined the test for EB-2 NIW eligibility. It replaced older, stricter criteria with a more flexible three-part test. You must prove all three prongs:

Prong 1: Merit Your proposed endeavor has substantial merit and national importance. Your field (aerospace engineering, climate research, space exploration, propulsion systems, etc.) must be important to the US. This is usually easy for aerospace engineers because NASA, space commerce, and defense are core national interests. Evidence: professional credentials, degrees, publications, patents, job responsibilities showing work in nationally important field.

Prong 2: Well Positioned to Advance You are well positioned to advance the proposed endeavor. Your credentials, experience, and expertise must demonstrate you can contribute meaningfully. Evidence: CV, publications, patents, letters of recommendation from colleagues, job offers, descriptions of your specific projects and contributions.

Prong 3: Waiver Benefiting US Granting the waiver (exempting you from labor certification requirement) is beneficial to the US. This means your work will benefit the US and the labor certification waiver will not harm US workers. Since you are in a specialized field with few competitors, this is usually provable. Evidence: letter from employer explaining your work's impact, letters from government agencies (NASA, DoD, NOAA) supporting your work, publications showing your contributions, job market analysis showing shortage of qualified professionals in your specialty.

Practical example: An aerospace engineer with a master's degree who has published research on hypersonic propulsion systems, holds patents in guidance systems, and works for a NASA contractor on a critical space mission can argue: (1) Hypersonic propulsion has substantial national importance (prong 1); (2) I have directly contributed to this field through research and patents (prong 2); (3) Waiving labor certification and allowing me to remain in the US serves national interest because few engineers have my specialized knowledge (prong 3).

Real Case Study: Emiliano Kargieman, Argentina to NASDAQ-Listed Space CEO

Emiliano Kargieman is an Argentine aerospace engineer who founded Satellogic in 2010. Satellogic is a leading Latin American space technology company focused on satellite Earth observation. The company launched its first satellite in 2015 and has since launched 50+ nanosatellites. In 2022, Satellogic went public on NASDAQ.

Emiliano's pathway: Argentine engineer, started a company in Buenos Aires, scaled it to US presence, eventually pursued US permanent residence while building the company. His story demonstrates that LATAM aerospace professionals do not have to work for NASA or major US contractors. They can build their own companies and use immigration pathways to scale operations.

Why mention Emiliano? Because EB-2 NIW is particularly attractive for entrepreneurs and innovators. If you have a startup or idea that serves national interest, you can petition for yourself. You do not have to wait for employer sponsorship.

ITAR (International Traffic in Arms Regulations): What You Must Know

What is ITAR? ITAR is a set of US government regulations (codified in the International Traffic in Arms Regulations, Title 22 Code of Federal Regulations, Part 120-130) that controls the export and transfer of defense and space-related technology. ITAR is managed by the US Department of State Directorate of Defense Trade Controls (DDTC).

Who is subject to ITAR? US persons are subject to ITAR. "US persons" includes: US citizens and lawful permanent residents (green card holders). Non-citizens without permanent residency (including F-1 students and H-1B visa holders) are NOT subject to ITAR, BUT companies may restrict them from accessing ITAR-controlled information because of compliance burden.

What work is ITAR-controlled? Any work involving design, development, manufacture, or use of defense articles or items on the US Munitions List (USML). For aerospace, this includes: satellite propulsion systems, guidance and navigation systems, launch vehicle technology, missile systems, military aircraft systems, spacecraft with defense applications. Many civilian space programs (like commercial satellite work) are also ITAR-controlled.

Critical implication: If you are an F-1 student or H-1B visa holder, you generally CANNOT work on ITAR projects. Many aerospace companies are ITAR-licensed, which means most of their work requires ITAR compliance. Your employment options at these companies may be restricted until you get a green card (permanent resident status).

NASA civil service vs. NASA contractor: NASA Goddard (where I work) is a federal civil service facility. Civil service positions require US citizenship. You CANNOT be a NASA civil service employee (astronaut, flight director, engineer in civil service role) without US citizenship. However, NASA contracts work out to contractors (Axiom Space, Northrop Grumman, Lockheed Martin, etc.). Contractors can employ H-1B visa holders and sometimes permanent residents on non-ITAR or ITAR-approved work. ITAR applies to contractors too, but contractor roles may have more flexibility than civil service.

How to navigate ITAR as a non-citizen:

  • During F-1 OPT: Target companies that do non-ITAR work or have non-ITAR divisions. Research the company's ITAR licensing status before accepting a job.
  • During H-1B: Ask your employer explicitly: "Does my role involve ITAR work?" If yes, ask what restrictions apply. Some companies may petition for green card sponsorship to allow ITAR work transition.
  • Once you have green card (permanent resident): You become a "US person" and can work on ITAR projects without restriction.

Red flag: If a company offers you a job at a high salary and then suddenly says you cannot work on ITAR after you start, you have a problem. Discuss ITAR explicitly before accepting offers.

Priority Dates and Visa Bulletin: Why LATAM Countries Have an Advantage

What is a priority date? For employment-based green cards, USCIS assigns a "priority date" based on when your employer or you file the initial application (I-140 for EB, or I-485 for certain categories). Your priority date determines when you can file your green card application.

What is visa bulletin? The US Department of State publishes a "visa bulletin" monthly showing which priority dates are currently "current" (meaning you can file or be approved). The bulletin varies by employment category and country of origin.

Country backlog: Some countries, notably India and China, have massive backlogs. Engineers from India can wait 5-10+ years for EB-2 green cards because so many Indian engineers are petitioning. China also has backlogs. However, most LATAM countries (Mexico, Peru, Colombia, Argentina, Brazil, Chile, Costa Rica, etc.) have minimal or no backlog. As of 2025, engineers from most LATAM countries can move through EB-2 green card process in 2-5 years, compared to 10+ years for Indian engineers.

Implication: If you are an aerospace engineer from LATAM and you pursue EB-2 green card (either NIW or PERM), you will likely get your green card 3-5 years after initiating. This is a major advantage and one reason why LATAM engineers should NOT hesitate to pursue permanent residence.

How to Find a Qualified Immigration Attorney

Why you need an attorney: Do not try to navigate visa sponsorship alone. Even one mistake can delay your case years or result in denial. Your attorney is your advocate, your advisor, and your insurance policy. The cost ($3,000-$10,000) is small compared to the value of permanent residence.

Where to find attorneys:

  • AILA (American Immigration Lawyers Association): www.ailalawyer.com. Search by location and specialization. AILA attorneys are vetted and ethical.
  • State bar association: Search "[your state] bar association immigration lawyers" to find attorneys licensed in your state.
  • Company HR: Ask your company HR for referrals. Large aerospace companies have relationships with immigration law firms.
  • Professional network: Ask colleagues and mentors for referrals to attorneys they have used.

What to look for in an attorney:

  • Licensed to practice immigration law (check bar admission).
  • Experience with employment-based green cards (EB-1, EB-2, H-1B).
  • Experience with your industry (aerospace, tech, research) is a plus.
  • Clear fee structure (hourly or flat fee).
  • Responsive communication (replies to emails/calls within 24-48 hours).
  • References or reviews from past clients.

Red flags:

  • "Notarios" or people claiming to be immigration consultants WITHOUT a law license. In many Latin American countries, "notario" means attorney. In the US, notaries are NOT attorneys and CANNOT provide legal advice. Notarios can commit fraud and ruin your case.
  • Attorneys offering guarantees ("100% approval guaranteed"). No attorney can guarantee outcomes.
  • Attorneys asking you to pay in cash or offshore accounts. Legitimate attorneys invoice normally.
  • Attorneys rushing you to file or pressuring you. Good attorneys explain options and let you decide.
  • Attorneys who do not respond to questions or seem disorganized.

Initial consultation: Most immigration attorneys offer a 30-minute initial consultation free or for a small fee ($100-300). Use this to: (1) Explain your situation and ask if the attorney can help; (2) Ask about fees and timeline; (3) Get a sense of whether you are comfortable with them. You should feel confident in your attorney.

You have come so far: F-1 to OPT to H-1B. The final step is green card. You can do this. Thousands of international aerospace engineers have done it. You will too.

Knowledge Check Quiz (5 questions)

Q1: Which green card category allows self-petitioning (no employer required)?
A) EB-2 PERM | B) EB-2 NIW (National Interest Waiver) | C) EB-3 | D) None; all require employers
Answer: B) EB-2 NIW (National Interest Waiver). EB-2 NIW is unique because you can self-petition. You do not need an employer. This is valuable for researchers, innovators, and professionals with advanced degrees who have contributed to nationally important work.

Q2: What is the three-prong test for EB-2 NIW called?
A) PERM Test | B) Matter of Dhanasar | C) USCIS Standard | D) National Interest Protocol
Answer: B) Matter of Dhanasar. Matter of Dhanasar is a 2016 USCIS precedent case that defined the current, more flexible test for EB-2 NIW. It requires proving: (1) merit/national importance, (2) well positioned to advance, (3) waiver benefits US.

Q3: Who can work on ITAR-controlled projects?
A) Any visa holder | B) F-1 students with CPT authorization | C) US persons (citizens and permanent residents) | D) H-1B visa holders
Answer: C) US persons (citizens and permanent residents). ITAR regulations restrict ITAR-controlled work to US persons. F-1 students, H-1B visa holders, and other non-citizens are legally barred from ITAR work. Permanent residents (green card holders) are considered US persons and can work on ITAR projects.

Q4: Can you work as a civil service astronaut or flight director at NASA Goddard without US citizenship?
A) Yes, with H-1B | B) Yes, with permanent residence | C) No, civil service requires citizenship | D) Yes, if the role is non-ITAR
Answer: C) No, civil service requires citizenship. NASA civil service positions (including astronauts, flight directors, civil service engineers) require US citizenship. Non-citizens can work for NASA contractors on contractor roles, but not in civil service.

Q5: Approximately how long does EB-2 green card sponsorship typically take for an engineer from Peru or Colombia?
A) 1-2 years | B) 2-5 years | C) 8-10 years | D) 15+ years
Answer: B) 2-5 years. Most LATAM countries (Peru, Colombia, Mexico, Argentina, Brazil, Chile, Costa Rica) have minimal backlog for EB-2 green cards. Processing typically takes 2-5 years from initial petition filing to green card approval. This is much faster than India or China, which can take 10-15+ years.
