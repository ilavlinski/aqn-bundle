Lesson 4.3: Conferences, Competitions, and Grants

Module 4: Research & Innovation Lab

The first time I gave an oral presentation at a conference, I was terrified. My grandmother in Peru couldn't afford to come, but I called her after and told her I had stood up in front of 200 scientists and talked about spacecraft in English. She cried. She told me, "No one in our family ever did this." That moment changed how I saw myself. I wasn't just a student doing work in a lab. I was a scientist with a voice. Conferences aren't just about sharing papers. They're about claiming your place in the scientific community. Right now, I want to show you how to do that, how to design a poster, give a talk, and start building the professional identity that will define your career for decades.

Video Script, Aracely / Irma reads on camera

VIDEO SCRIPT:

"Conferences are where science happens outside of papers. You meet researchers, you get feedback on your work in real time, you see what questions are trending in your field. And crucially, people meet you. Your face, your voice, your ideas matter.

There are two main ways to present: poster or oral. A poster is a large printed board (usually 4 feet by 6 feet) with your abstract, figures, and key findings. You stand by it during a session, and people walk by and ask questions. An oral talk is 10-20 minutes on stage with slides. Both are valuable; posters are better for first-time presenters because you control the pace of conversation.

The conferences that matter for researchers depend on your field. In earth science, it's the American Geophysical Union (AGU), this meeting has 25,000 people every December in San Francisco. If you're in aeronautics or space systems, it's AIAA, the American Institute of Aeronautics and Astronautics. If you're underrepresented in science, and many of you are, there's SACNAS, the Society for Advancement of Chicanos and Native Americans in Science. SACNAS has hundreds of workshops, mentoring, and a culture that says, 'You belong here.'

Presenting at conferences isn't just academic. It's personal. When you present, you're saying, 'I did work. I have knowledge. I'm part of this conversation.' That's powerful, especially if you're first-generation or from a community that hasn't traditionally been represented in STEM. Building your professional identity now, in your 20s, while you're learning, shapes everything that comes after.

Let me show you how."

Finding & Selecting the Right Conference

By Field:

  • Earth Science / Climate / Atmospheric: AGU (American Geophysical Union) Fall Meeting (December, San Francisco, largest earth science conference), EGU (European Geophysical Union, April, Vienna), or AMS (American Meteorological Society).
  • Planetary Science: AAS Division for Planetary Sciences (annual), or smaller: Lunar and Planetary Science Conference (LPSC, March, Houston).
  • Aeronautics / Aerospace / Propulsion: AIAA (American Institute of Aeronautics and Astronautics) holds multiple conferences per year. Start with regional or student-focused meetings.
  • Underrepresented in STEM (Hispanic, Native American, Black, First-Gen): SACNAS (National Diversity in STEM Conference, October, rotating locations). This is not just about research, it's about building community and mentorship.
  • Multidisciplinary / Broad Science: National Council of Teachers of Science (NSTA), American Chemical Society (ACS) if you do chemistry-adjacent work, or your institution's graduate symposium.

When to Submit

Most major conferences open calls for abstracts 4-6 months before the meeting. Example: AGU Fall Meeting (December) usually opens abstracts in March. You have about 2 weeks to submit. Set a calendar alert for when calls open. Many conferences have early-bird student discounts if you submit early.

Cost to Attend

Conference fees are $200-$800 depending on size and prestige. Hotels and travel add another $800-$2,000. This sounds expensive, but:

  • Your advisor's grant often covers it. Ask when you submit your abstract.
  • Student registrations are typically 50 percent off.
  • Many conferences offer travel grants or scholarships for underrepresented groups. SACNAS, AGU, and AIAA all have funding programs.
  • Some events have "virtual attendance" for reduced cost if travel is impossible.

Preparing a Research Poster

Technical Specifications

Most conferences specify: 4 feet wide x 6 feet tall (some are 4x8). Check the conference guidelines. Orientation is typically portrait. You'll print this as a single large file (not multiple pages taped together).

Content Layout (Top to Bottom):

  • Title (top, largest font, 54-72pt): Your project name. Make it declarative: "Modeling Propellant Vortex Dynamics in Low-Gravity Engines" not "Research on Rocket Fuel."
  • Author line (below title, 18-24pt): Your name, institution, email. If it's collaboration, list all authors and institutions.
  • Introduction / Background (left column, 24pt body text): Why does this matter? What's the gap in knowledge? 2-3 paragraphs.
  • Methods (left or middle column, 24pt): What did you do? 1-2 paragraphs, maybe one schematic diagram.
  • Results (right column, 24pt + large figures): Figures take up 40-50 percent of poster space. 3-5 high-quality graphs or images. Each with clear caption.
  • Discussion / Conclusions (bottom right, 24pt): What does it mean? Future work? 2-3 sentences.
  • References (small, 14-16pt): Minimum four citations showing you know the context.

Design Tips (Not Decoration):

  • Use readable fonts: Arial, Helvetica, or Calibri. No Comic Sans, no script fonts.
  • High contrast: Dark text on light background (or light on dark if you prefer). Avoid light gray on white.
  • Color use: Use color strategically to separate sections. Don't rainbow-color every element. Maximum 2-3 colors plus black/white.
  • Figures are the star: Your figures should dominate. Someone walking past should understand your main finding from figures alone. Captions matter more than text.
  • Whitespace: Don't cram text. Leave breathing room. Dense posters are hard to read.
  • One message: Your poster should convey one clear finding. "We found that X is true because of Y." If it's more complex, you're trying to fit too much.

Tools for Making a Poster

  • Microsoft PowerPoint (landscape orientation, set custom size to 48in x 72in)
  • Canva (has conference poster templates)
  • Adobe Illustrator (professional but steeper learning curve)
  • LaTeX + TikZ (for highly technical posters; overkill for most)

Printing

Most universities have printing services that handle large-format posters. Cost is typically $30-$60. Send your file 1-2 weeks before the conference. Bring it rolled (not folded) in your luggage.

Giving an Oral Talk

Talk Length & Timing

Oral presentations are usually 10-20 minutes, plus 5 minutes for questions. For your first talk, aim for 12 minutes of content. Practice the timing relentlessly. Rushing signals nervousness; dragging bores people.

Slide Structure (for a 12-minute talk):

  • Title slide (1 min): Title, authors, institution, date. Use this while you introduce yourself.
  • Context / Why This Matters (2 min, 2-3 slides): Explain the problem to someone outside your exact sub-field. "Before our work, scientists didn't know X. We set out to measure Y."
  • Methods (2-3 min, 2-3 slides): How did you do it? One diagram explaining your approach helps enormously.
  • Results (5 min, 4-5 slides): Show your key figures. One figure per slide. Spend 1+ minute on your most important result.
  • Conclusion / Implications (1-2 min, 1-2 slides): So what? What comes next? Why should the audience care?

Slide Design Principles

  • One idea per slide. If a slide has three separate concepts, split it into three slides.
  • Minimal text. Slides support your talk; they're not the talk. Use bullet points, not paragraphs. Audience should read your slides in 3-5 seconds, then listen to you explain.
  • Large figures. Charts should be 50-70 percent of the slide. Zoom in so the audience can read axis labels from the back of the room.
  • Readable fonts. 28pt minimum for body text, 44pt for titles. Test by standing 10 feet away from your screen.
  • Avoid transitions and animations. They distract. A simple fade or cut between slides is fine; spinning titles are not.
  • Consistent branding. Same background, same color scheme on every slide. Looks professional.

Delivering the Talk

  • Know your opening sentence by heart. Memorize the first 1-2 minutes. After that, you'll be less nervous and can speak more naturally.
  • Make eye contact. Look at people in the audience, not your slides. Your laptop screen is in front of you; use it as a reference, not a script.
  • Speak clearly and slowly. English as a second language? Embrace your accent. Speak slowly so every word is understood. Pauses are your friend. Don't fill silence with "um."
  • Use the laser pointer sparingly. Point at key figures, then lower it. Don't let it distract.
  • Expect questions; welcome them. "Thanks for that question. Let me think... what I found was that X increased by 30 percent because of Y." You don't need to know everything. "That's a great point I hadn't considered. I'd like to think about that more" is a valid answer.
  • Practice in front of people. Do a "practice talk" in your lab group, to your advisor, to friends. Get feedback on clarity, pacing, and timing. This is non-negotiable.

Building Your Professional Identity in STEM

What Does Professional Identity Mean?

It's how people perceive you in your field. Are you known as the person who studies X? As reliable? As innovative? As approachable? As a mentor? Your name carries weight. Right now, you're building that reputation. Every paper, every conference talk, every email to a colleague is part of it.

Why It Matters for Latinx and Underrepresented Scientists

Study after study shows that women and people from underrepresented communities are asked to prove their competence more than white men. That's unfair, but it's true. Building a visible professional identity, publishing papers, giving talks, serving on committees, makes it harder for people to dismiss or overlook you. Your work becomes undeniable.

Practical Identity-Building Steps

  • Attend conferences, especially SACNAS. You meet mentors, peers, industry people. These relationships sustain careers.
  • Join professional societies. AIAA membership is $80/year and gets you journal access, meeting discounts, and networking. SACNAS membership costs less and includes mentoring programs.
  • Get on social media (LinkedIn, X/Twitter, or Instagram). Share one finding from your research every few months. A one-sentence observation about your work. This takes 5 minutes and makes you visible.
  • Mentor younger students. Once you've been in research for 6-12 months, mentor someone newer. Teaching solidifies your knowledge and builds your reputation as someone who lifts others up. This is especially important in LATAM communities where family support systems aren't everywhere.
  • Be intentional about representation. If you're in a room full of white men, your presence matters. If you're the only woman, the only Hispanic person, the only first-gen student, you represent something. Own that. Not as burden, as power.

The SACNAS Example

SACNAS (Society for Advancement of Chicanos and Native Americans in Science) holds an annual conference with 2,000+ scientists, students, and professionals. There's research presentations (like any conference), but also: mentoring sessions, family-oriented events (because many Latinx scientists care about family), social events, awards for underrepresented scientists. The culture says explicitly: "You belong here." That's rare and powerful. Attend at least once before you graduate.

LATAM Success Story: Rodolfo Neri Vela

Rodolfo Neri Vela, a Mexican physicist and engineer, flew on Space Shuttle Atlantis (STS-61-B) in 1985, the first Mexican-born person in space. Before his shuttle flight, he was a research scientist at the National University of Mexico (UNAM), publishing work on telecommunications. His professional identity was built through research, not just astronaut selection. He demonstrated that Mexicans could do top-tier science and engineering. That visibility opened doors for generations. His work as a researcher preceded his spaceflight; research was foundational.

LATAM Success Story: Serena Auñón-Chancellor

Serena Auñón-Chancellor, of Cuban heritage, is a physician and astronaut who spent 197 days on the International Space Station (2018). Before her spaceflight, she built a professional identity as a medical researcher and physician, not just an astronaut candidate. She published work on space medicine, mentored medical students, and spoke publicly about the importance of diversity in space exploration. Her visible professional work made her competitive for astronaut selection. Now she continues to mentor, especially young Latina scientists. Her voice in the scientific community extends far beyond her time in orbit.

Practice Exercise

Design a Conference Poster (90 minutes):

  1. Choose a research project (yours or a published paper you admire). If you have no research yet, use a published paper from Google Scholar in your field of interest.
  2. Using PowerPoint, Canva, or another tool, create a 4x6-foot poster with title, abstract, methods, results, and conclusion. Don't worry about polish, focus on layout.
  3. Have someone else (not you) look at it for 30 seconds. Ask them: "What's the main finding?" If they can't answer from the poster alone, your figure layout needs work.
  4. Submit the poster to your advisor or a mentor for feedback. Iterate once based on their input.

Quiz: Conference Presentations & Professional Identity

Question 1: Which major conference in earth science is attended by roughly 25,000 scientists each December?
A) AIAA Annual Conference
B) American Geophysical Union (AGU) Fall Meeting
C) SACNAS National Diversity in STEM Conference
D) Lunar and Planetary Science Conference (LPSC)
Answer: B. AGU Fall Meeting (December, San Francisco) is the largest earth science conference. LPSC is planetary-specific but smaller. AIAA serves aeronautics/aerospace. SACNAS is multidisciplinary but smaller.

Question 2: What is the ideal font size for body text on a research poster?
A) 12-16 point (standard reading size)
B) 18-20 point
C) 24+ point
D) 8-10 point (like in papers)
Answer: C. Posters are read from 3-6 feet away. 24+ point ensures readability. Smaller fonts defeat the purpose of a poster's visibility.

Question 3: For a 12-minute oral presentation, how many minutes should you spend on your most important results?
A) 1 minute or less
B) 2-3 minutes
C) 5-7 minutes
D) The entire talk
Answer: C. Your key finding deserves 40-50 percent of talk time. Methods and context together take 4-5 minutes; results take 5-6 minutes; conclusions take 1-2 minutes.

Question 4: What is one key advantage of attending SACNAS (Society for Advancement of Chicanos and Native Americans in Science)?
A) It's the cheapest conference to attend
B) You're guaranteed job placement
C) It explicitly centers mentoring, community, and the message that underrepresented scientists belong, alongside research presentations
D) You don't have to present research
Answer: C. SACNAS uniquely combines research with explicit mentoring, community building, and family inclusion. Many students report that SACNAS is the first conference where they felt fully welcome and seen.

Question 5: Why is building a professional identity in STEM important, especially for Latinx and underrepresented scientists?
A) It's optional, you can still get hired without it
B) It makes you seem arrogant
C) It helps counter unconscious bias by making your work and contributions visible and undeniable
D) It only matters for people pursuing leadership
Answer: C. Visible professional work (papers, talks, mentoring, community involvement) makes you and your contributions harder to overlook or dismiss. This is particularly important given research showing increased scrutiny of underrepresented scientists.

You are building something larger than a CV. You're claiming a place in a global scientific community that for too long didn't look like you. When you present at a conference, when you publish a paper, when you mentor someone younger, you're saying: "I'm here. My work matters. People who look like me and come from places like me belong in science." That's not arrogance. That's truth. And the world needs that from you.
