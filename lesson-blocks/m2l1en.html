Video Lesson: 90-Day English Roadmap

Watch Aracely share her English learning path and walk you through the three-phase strategy (approximately 8 minutes).

Video Script (for Aracely)

Hi. I'm Aracely Quispe Neira, and I want to tell you something that might surprise you. When I arrived in the United States at eighteen years old from Lambayeque, Peru, I spoke almost no English. Not a little bit. Almost none. I had studied it in school, sure, but the English I knew was grammar textbook English, memorized verb conjugations. It was not the English of daily life, of technical meetings, of survival.

I came here to study engineering. I was accepted to a good program, but there was a problem. My classes were entirely in English. My professors spoke fast. American professors do not slow down for international students. My engineering textbooks were in English. The library was in English. The study groups were in English. I sat in lectures understanding maybe forty percent. I went home and cried.

That was 1999. I did not have YouTube. I did not have iTalki. I did not have language apps. I had a dictionary, a lot of stubbornness, and a grandmother's voice in my head saying, "Mija, there is always a way." So I built my own system. I will share it with you because I know that what I did then, refined now with modern tools, will work for you.

The first thing you need to understand is why English matters in aerospace. It is not just nice to have. It is essential. Every major aerospace organization on Earth operates in English. NASA operates in English. SpaceX operates in English. The European Space Agency operates in English. When astronauts from ten different countries work on the International Space Station, they communicate in English. When engineers write specifications for rockets, they write in English. When you sit in a technical interview with a NASA flight director, you will be interviewed in English.

The data backs this up. Engineers with strong English certification earn on average twenty-eight percent higher salaries across aerospace, technology, and energy. That is not a small number. That is a house, a car, student loan forgiveness. The most valuable engineers in aerospace are not just brilliant. They are brilliant and they can communicate. They can write emails that are clear. They can present technical ideas in meetings without hesitation. They can understand native speakers on video calls and not feel lost.

When you apply for a NASA clearance, they test your English. When you apply for an H-1B visa, they want documented English proficiency. When you move into leadership at any aerospace company, you are managing meetings in English. Writing proposals in English. Mentoring junior engineers in English. Your accent does not matter. Your clarity matters. Your confidence matters.

So here is the plan. It is a ninety-day plan because ninety days is achievable. It is not a year. It is not five years. It is a quarter. If you commit fully to these three phases, in ninety days your English will be transformed. You will understand more. You will speak with more confidence. You will be ready to take the TOEFL or IELTS and score high enough to matter.

Phase One is Foundation. Days one through thirty. Your job in this phase is to fill your ears with English. You are building what linguists call receptive vocabulary. You are not trying to speak yet. You are listening. You are absorbing the rhythm of English, the intonation, the accent of aerospace professionals.

I want you to listen to aerospace podcasts and YouTube channels every single day. Scott Manley. Everyday Astronaut. NASA's official channels. Listen while you commute. Listen while you cook. Listen while you exercise. One to two hours daily. Do not take notes. Do not pause and look up words. Just let it flow into your ears. Your brain is more powerful than you think. It will pick up patterns. It will learn.

At the same time, hire a tutor through iTalki. Two sessions per week, thirty minutes each. Find a tutor with aerospace background or TOEFL teaching experience. Your tutor's job is not to teach you grammar. It is to listen to you speak and correct your pronunciation and your patterns. The tutor is your feedback mirror. You will stumble. You will make mistakes. The tutor catches them and shows you the right way.

Use Anki for vocabulary. Anki is a flashcard tool. It uses something called spaced repetition. Every card you see, Anki knows when you are most likely to forget it. It shows it to you just before you forget. You can find pre-built aerospace decks online. Twenty minutes daily. Focus on the words that matter in your field. Not just common English. Technical English.

By the end of Phase One, you should understand seventy percent of technical aerospace YouTube videos without subtitles. Not perfectly. But enough that you catch the main ideas and the vocabulary starts feeling familiar.

Phase Two is Production. Days thirty-one through sixty. Now you start speaking more. You use the Tandem app. Tandem connects you with native English speakers and near-native speakers from around the world. You have thirty-minute conversations in English every single day. You talk about aerospace. You talk about your engineering background. You talk about your dreams. You stumble. You laugh. You try again.

You also escalate your iTalki tutoring. Four sessions per week now instead of two. You are speaking more. You are getting feedback more. You are refining your accent and your sentence structures. You are building muscle memory in your mouth for English sounds that might not exist in Spanish.

In this phase, start writing. Medium. Dev.to. Write one technical post per week. Three hundred words about an aerospace topic you love. Get your tutor to read it. Get feedback. Rewrite it. This forces you to think in English, not translate from Spanish.

By the end of Phase Two, you should be able to give a five to ten-minute technical presentation on your field with minimal hesitation. Not perfectly. But with growing confidence. You are no longer performing English. You are becoming an English speaker.

Phase Three is Technical and Certification. Days sixty-one through ninety. Now it is exam time if you need a formal credential. TOEFL iBT or IELTS Academic. Take the official ETS practice tests. Every three days, take a full-length practice exam. This is not fun. This is work. But this is what prepares you to score high.

At the same time, drill specialized aerospace vocabulary. Propellant grain. Avionics suite. Gimbal control. Aerothermal loads. These are the words that will appear in technical interviews. Thirty minutes of Anki daily focused on advanced aerospace terms.

Schedule mock interview practice through iTalki. Hire a tutor to ask you NASA-style and SpaceX-style questions. Technical interviews in aerospace are not about being perfect. They are about showing that you can think under pressure and communicate your thinking clearly.

By Day Ninety, you will take the official TOEFL iBT or IELTS exam. You will have your score in hand. Most universities and employers recognize TOEFL scores over one hundred as strong evidence of English fluency. Most sponsorships and clearances want IELTS at seven-point-zero or higher. This exam is not the finish line. It is a checkpoint. It proves to employers and visa officers that you can do this.

I know this sounds intense. It is. Language is not learned passively. It is learned through deliberate practice, repetition, and courage to fail in front of people. But I did it without YouTube. Without apps. Without iTalki. You have all the tools. You have no excuses.

Your accent will probably not disappear. Mine did not. I have a Costa Rican colleague at NASA named Franklin Chang-Díaz. He is a PhD physicist from MIT. He leads major projects. He speaks with a Costa Rican accent. No one cares. They care that he is brilliant and he can articulate his ideas clearly.

So here is my challenge to you. Pick today as Day One. Download Anki tonight. Watch a NASA YouTube video for thirty minutes. Schedule your first iTalki session. Tomorrow, do it again. On Day Thirty, you will be amazed at yourself. On Day Sixty, you will be unstoppable. On Day Ninety, you will be ready.

English is not a barrier. English is your rocket fuel. It is the thing that launches your career into orbit. Let's go.

The multiplier effect: Strong English transforms you from "qualified engineer" to "strategic asset." Companies compete for bilingual talent who can bridge LATAM markets and communicate with global teams.

The 90-Day Plan: Three Phases to Fluency

English fluency is not an accident. It is a project with phases, milestones, and measurable output. Here is the structure that works.

Phase 1: Foundation (Days 1-30)

Goal: Build receptive vocabulary and passive comprehension of aerospace English. Target: understand 70% of technical YouTube videos without subtitles.

  • Shadowing technique (1-2 hours daily): Listen to aerospace content every single day. NASA Johnson Space Center channel. Everyday Astronaut. Scott Manley. Real Engineers React series. Do NOT take notes. Do NOT pause to look up words. Let your brain absorb rhythm, intonation, and technical accent naturally.
  • Live tutoring (2 sessions per week, 30 minutes each): Schedule sessions through iTalki with tutors who have aerospace background or TOEFL certification. Focus on pronunciation correction and listening comprehension feedback. Budget: 10-15 dollars per week.
  • Anki spaced repetition (20 minutes daily): Start with "Aerospace English 1000" pre-built deck available on Anki.com. Focus on verbs, nouns, and collocations used in aerospace. Spaced repetition ensures you remember what matters.
  • Passive exposure: Change your phone language to English. Watch aerospace documentaries with subtitles. Read aerospace Reddit threads. Read NASA blog posts even if you understand only half. Your brain learns from context.

Phase 1 output: By Day 30, you understand main ideas in technical aerospace videos. You can recognize 500+ aerospace-specific English words. You have a tutor relationship established and daily study routine locked in.

Phase 2: Production (Days 31-60)

Goal: Build speaking fluency and writing accuracy for technical communication. Target: deliver a 5-10 minute technical presentation with minimal hesitation.

  • Daily speaking practice via Tandem (30 minutes daily): Use the Tandem language exchange app to talk with native speakers and near-native speakers. Seek out aerospace engineers, pilots, and space enthusiasts. Speak about your background, your interests, your projects. Stumble. Laugh. Try again. Cost: 5-8 dollars per week. Free version available but limited.
  • Tutoring intensification (4 sessions per week, 30-45 minutes each): Move to 4 tutoring sessions per week on iTalki. Hire tutors with aerospace or TOEFL teaching background. Speak your field. Practice technical storytelling. Record yourself and listen back to identify patterns. Budget: 40-60 dollars per week.
  • Technical writing (1 post per week, 300 words): Start a blog on Medium, Dev.to, or Hashnode. Write one post per week about an aerospace topic you love. Topics: "How Gimbal Control Works," "Why Rocket Bodies Fall Slower Than You Think," "The Physics of Orbital Mechanics." Get your tutor to review for grammar and clarity. Rewrite based on feedback.
  • Speaking milestones: By Day 45, record a 5-minute explanation of a technical concept in your field. By Day 60, present that same topic live to your iTalki tutor and ask for feedback on delivery, not just grammar.

Phase 2 output: By Day 60, you are conversational in aerospace English. You can explain technical ideas. You can ask questions in meetings. You can handle basic professional communication. You are starting to think in English instead of translating from Spanish.

Phase 3: Technical and Certification (Days 61-90)

Goal: Master specialized aerospace vocabulary and pass formal TOEFL iBT or IELTS exam with strong score. Target: TOEFL 100+ or IELTS 7.0+ to prove fluency to employers and visa officers.

  • TOEFL iBT or IELTS preparation (practice exam every 3 days): Download official ETS TOEFL iBT practice tests. Take full-length practice exams every three days. This phase is exam-focused and intense. You are not learning English anymore. You are learning how to perform English under time pressure. Cost: 25 dollars per practice test, approximately 150 dollars for 6 tests.
  • Advanced aerospace vocabulary (30 minutes daily Anki): Create or find advanced aerospace Anki deck. Focus on technical collocations and specialized terms: "propellant grain," "avionics suite," "gimbal control," "aerothermal loads," "structural analysis," "thermal protection system," "guidance and navigation." These are the words that appear in technical interviews.
  • Mock interviews (2-3 sessions, 30-45 minutes each): Schedule mock interview practice through iTalki with tutors experienced in technical interviews. Ask NASA-style technical questions. SpaceX-style behavioral questions. Practice thinking and speaking under mild pressure. Record sessions and review for clarity and pacing.
  • Exam date lock-in: Schedule your official TOEFL iBT or IELTS Academic exam for Day 90. This creates accountability. You cannot skip. The exam forces you to perform.

Phase 3 output: By Day 90, you have an official TOEFL iBT score (100+) or IELTS Academic score (7.0+). You have mastered aerospace technical vocabulary. You can handle technical interviews with growing confidence. You have proof in hand that you are a serious English speaker.

Why Latin American Engineers Struggle with English and How to Avoid It

Your advantage is mathematical and technical rigor. Your challenge is speaking confidence and exam pressure. Here is what trips up Spanish speakers:

  • Cognate confidence trap: "Hospital" looks like "hospital." You skip grammar checking. But aerospace demands precision. "Pressure vessel" is not "presión vasija." False cognates are dangerous. Study these: "constipated" (not constipado), "embarrassed" (not embarazada), "actually" (not actualmente), "success" (not suceso).
  • Pronunciation shyness: Spanish speakers often whisper English sounds. Aerospace requires confident, clear communication. Practice volume and clarity deliberately. Speak louder than feels natural. Your voice matters.
  • Exam timing anxiety: TOEFL reading is timed. Spanish readers process English text slower due to language processing overhead. Practice timed sections starting from Day 30. You need speed endurance, not just comprehension.
  • Presentation anxiety: You understand more than you speak. This gap creates anxiety. Practice speaking BEFORE you feel ready. Discomfort equals growth zone. Record yourself and listen back. Feedback is love.
  • Accent preservation: Franklin Chang-Díaz, a Costa Rican-born MIT PhD and NASA astronaut who leads major aerospace programs, speaks with a clear Costa Rican accent. Your accent is not a barrier. Clarity is. Confidence is. Stop trying to sound American. Sound like yourself, clearly.
