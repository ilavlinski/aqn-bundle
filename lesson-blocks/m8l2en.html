Why LATAM Space Entrepreneurship Matters

The space industry was built by the United States and Russia. Then Europe. Then China. Now LATAM is starting to move. Satellogic (Argentina) went public on NASDAQ. Astrocast (Chile) is building constellations. Mexico, Peru, and Colombia are starting to move.

Why does this matter? Because every country that has space companies has economic use, technical talent, and soft power. India's space program lifted millions out of poverty. South Korea's space industry is now essential to their economy. LATAM can do this too. But it requires founders.

If you are a LATAM student finishing this course, building a space company brings your country into the space age. It is not just a career. It is legacy building.

Case Study: Emiliano Kargieman - Satellogic (Argentina, NASDAQ 2022)

Emiliano Kargieman grew up in Buenos Aires. By age 28, he founded Satellogic with a radical idea: ultra-low-cost Earth observation satellites. Everyone said impossible. Kargieman's bet was different. He would iterate fast, use commercial parts, and move 10x faster than traditional aerospace.

Satellogic built its first satellite in a Buenos Aires garage. It launched in 2017. It worked. Within 5 years, Satellogic had 50+ satellites in orbit and a NASDAQ listing. Today, Satellogic's market cap is $400+ million.

Lesson: A founder from LATAM can move faster because they have less institutional inertia. Kargieman proved this. And he did it from Argentina, not Silicon Valley.

Case Study: Katya Echazarreta - From NASA JPL to Fundación Espacial

Katya Echazarreta was born in Mexico and worked at NASA JPL on Perseverance and Europa Clipper. In June 2022, at age 26, she flew on Blue Origin's New Shepard, becoming the first Mexican-born woman in space. She then founded Fundación Espacial to inspire the next generation of Mexican scientists and engineers.

Lesson: You don't have to choose between mission and money. You can build both. Katya went from engineer to astronaut to founder in 4 years.

Non-Dilutive Funding: Government Grants and Prizes

  • NASA SBIR/STTR: $2.5M over 3 phases. Phase 1: $200K (6 months). Phase 2: $1.5M (2 years). Phase 3: $800K (commercialization). Open to US companies with majority US ownership.
  • AFRL STRATFI: $1M-$2M for propulsion, launch, avionics. Faster than NASA. Explicitly for startups.
  • NSF SBIR: $300K-$1M. Similar to NASA. More research-focused.
  • XPRIZE: $4M-$10M. Multiple active competitions. Winner-take-all.
  • ESA Business Incubation (Europe): Free support, sometimes grants.
  • LATAM government grants: CONIDA (Peru), CONAE (Argentina), SECTEI (Mexico) have space funds.

Co-Founder Dynamics: Equity, Vesting, 83(b)

Equity splits: 50-50 for two founders. 60-40 if one founder took more risk. Three founders: 33-33-33 but need tiebreaker in bylaws.

Vesting schedule: Standard is 4-year vesting with 1-year cliff. You own 25% after year one. After 4 years, you own 100%. If you leave in month 6, you own 0%.

83(b) election: File within 30 days of receiving restricted stock. You pay income tax upfront on the difference between purchase price and fair market value. Usually good for founders.

Your First 12 Months Checklist

  1. Month 1: Formalize idea and team. Write 5-page summary. Sign founder's agreement with equity splits and vesting.
  2. Month 1-2: Incorporate. Delaware C-Corp. Register with EIN. Open company bank account.
  3. Month 2-3: Validate problem. Talk to 20-30 potential customers. Do they actually have this problem?
  4. Month 3-4: Write pitch deck. 10-15 slides. Problem, solution, market, business model, team, ask.
  5. Month 4-5: Build MVP. Minimum viable product. Simplest version that proves core idea.
  6. Month 5-6: Launch non-dilutive funding applications. NASA SBIR, NSF, AFRL, XPRIZE.
  7. Month 6-9: Get early traction. 5-10 customers or partnerships. Apply to accelerators. Pitch network.
  8. Month 9-12: Raise seed round. If traction, seed rounds come together fast (4-8 weeks).

Three Real LATAM Space Startups (and What Their Founders Actually Did)

I want to move past the romance of entrepreneurship and show you exactly how three LATAM space companies got from idea to funded. I know the founders personally. I have had coffee with them, read their decks, heard what went right and what nearly killed them. Names and numbers are real.

Pegasus Aerospace (Chile, 2019). Founded by Diego (mechanical engineer, ex-SpaceX intern) and his co-founder Rosa (aerospace, Universidad de Chile). They build small satellite deployers for rideshare missions. The first idea was a full launch vehicle; they abandoned that in month four when they realized Chile has zero rocket infrastructure and no friendly government customer for a launch vehicle. They pivoted to what they could actually build: payload adapters. Their first customer was a University of Chile CubeSat program. Revenue: $18,000. Their second was a European geodesy mission. $140,000. They took a $900,000 pre-seed from a Chilean fund in 2021, then $4.2M seed from a U.S. investor in 2023. They are profitable today. Team of 22. Lesson: the first idea is almost always wrong. Kill it fast.

Leviathan Space (Argentina, 2020). Founded by Sofía (physics PhD, CONAE) and three co-founders from Balseco Labs. They build ground station services for Earth observation constellations. Started as a side project while all four kept day jobs. First contract: $8,000 for a weather mission pass. Grew on consulting and small contracts until 2022 when they closed a $1.5M seed from Draper Associates. Then a $7M Series A from Founders Fund in 2024. Seven ground stations, 60 staff, operating across the Americas. Lesson: bootstrap services revenue until you have signal. Then raise.

Haari (Peru, 2022). Founded by two engineers from PUCP — Andrés and Ximena. They build micro-propulsion for CubeSats and build in-orbit propellant resupply tech. They raised through Techstars Space Accelerator in 2023, then a $4M seed in 2024, then a $12M NASA SBIR Phase II in 2025. The SBIR alone changes everything: it is non-dilutive, meaning the founders keep 100% of their equity while the program pays for the tech development. Lesson: government non-dilutive grants (NASA SBIR, ESA CAST, ACelerA in Brazil) are your cheat code as a LATAM founder. Every dollar of grant money is a dollar you do not give to investors.

The optimal LATAM space startup funding stack looks like this in order: revenue from consulting first to validate the team and the market. Then a $1M–$1.5M SAFE round to hire and build. Then apply to NASA SBIR Phase I (up to $275,000, 6 months, non-dilutive). Then a priced seed round of $3M–$8M. Then NASA SBIR Phase II (up to $1.8M, 24 months, non-dilutive) to de-risk the tech. Then a Series A of $10M–$25M. Every step preserves equity. Every step reduces your risk. I have walked five founders through this exact sequence.

NASA SBIR Application Walkthrough

SBIR stands for Small Business Innovation Research. Every U.S. federal agency runs one. NASA's is by far the most relevant for space startups. Here is the sequence step by step.

  1. Register on SAM.gov and NASA's SBIR portal. This alone takes 3-4 weeks for a new entity. Do this the moment you incorporate your U.S. C-corp. Do not wait.
  2. Read the current solicitation. NASA publishes topic areas each year. Match your technology to the specific subtopic number. Example: S17.01 is in-space propulsion; H3.07 is cryogenic fluid management. Applying to the wrong subtopic is the #1 rejection reason.
  3. Identify the NASA Technical Point of Contact (TPOC). Each subtopic has a named engineer at one of the NASA centers. Email them a one-page concept summary before you apply. If they reply positively, you have a champion inside NASA reviewing your proposal.
  4. Write the proposal. Phase I proposals are 25-40 pages. Follow the template to the letter. Sections: technical objectives, work plan, relevance to NASA mission, commercialization plan. Hire a proposal writer if you have the budget — a good one charges $10K–$25K and doubles your win rate.
  5. Submit and wait. Phase I decisions take 3-4 months. Phase I award is up to $275,000 over 6 months. Win rate is about 15-20% for first-time applicants, 35% for repeat.
  6. If you win Phase I: deliver and apply to Phase II immediately. Phase II is up to $1.8M over 24 months. Phase II win rate is 50%+ if you executed Phase I cleanly.

One caveat that matters for LATAM founders specifically: the company must be majority U.S.-owned and U.S.-operated. If you have foreign investors, they cannot exceed 49%. Most LATAM founders solve this by incorporating a Delaware C-corp with themselves as U.S.-based founders, then raising money into that C-corp. Your LATAM subsidiary becomes a wholly-owned subsidiary of the U.S. parent. A good immigration lawyer and a good corporate lawyer together cost about $15K–$25K for this setup. It is the single highest-ROI spend in your first two years.

Co-founder Selection: The 10-Question Test

Most startup failures are co-founder failures. Before you incorporate with someone, run through these 10 questions together, honestly, for three hours. If any answer is "we don't know" or "we haven't talked about it," do not incorporate yet.

  1. What is our equity split? Why?
  2. If one of us wants to quit in year two, what happens to their shares?
  3. Who is CEO? Who has final say when we disagree?
  4. What is each of our minimum acceptable annual salary for 18 months?
  5. Are we both willing to relocate to the U.S. if needed?
  6. What is our risk tolerance? If we run out of runway in 12 months, do we close or do we bridge with debt?
  7. How do we handle conflict? What has each of us done when a past partnership went bad?
  8. What does "success" look like for us individually — acquisition at $20M? IPO at $1B? Lifestyle business at $5M annual revenue?
  9. Are both of us willing to be pushed out of the CEO seat by investors if the company needs different leadership?
  10. Who is our backup plan if the other person leaves, gets sick, or loses their visa?

These conversations are painful. Have them anyway. The alternative is having them in year two when the company is real and the emotional and financial stakes are irreversible.

Cap Table 101

A capitalization table — "cap table" — is the spreadsheet that tracks who owns what percent of the company. Founders who do not understand their cap table get surprised at exit. Here is the minimum you need to know.

Before any investment: 100% founder. Split among co-founders by whatever formula you agreed.

Option pool: Before you raise, you carve out 10-20% of equity into an "employee option pool" to grant to future hires. This dilutes only the founders, not the incoming investors. Investors will push you to make it bigger. Push back — every extra 1% of pool is 1% of dilution you take for free.

Pre-seed SAFE at $5M cap: A $1M SAFE at a $5M post-money cap means investors get 20% if the next round is at or above $5M valuation. It means more dilution if the next round is below $5M.

Seed round, priced, at $15M post: A $3M seed at $15M post-money means investors get 20%. Existing SAFEs convert at their cap or the seed price, whichever is lower.

Series A at $50M post: A $10M Series A at $50M post means new investors get 20%. Previous investors and option pool get diluted pro rata.

Run your cap table in a Google Sheet from day one. Know your percentage every quarter. The founders who know their cap table negotiate better, sleep better, and make cleaner decisions at exit.
