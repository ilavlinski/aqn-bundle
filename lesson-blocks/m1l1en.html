A Girl from Marripón

I grew up in Marripón, a small town in Lambayeque, northern Peru. My abuela lived with us. She didn't speak much about her own ambitions, but she moved through the world with a kind of quiet certainty. My mother worked hard, and I watched her do it. I watched the two of them every day making decisions, making things work, making space for me and my brother to study. There was no money for tutors or fancy prep classes. There was just books, the dinner table, and time. When I was young, I spent hours on our porch looking up. Peru has dark skies in the north. You can see so much more there. I didn't know then what space meant. I just knew it existed, and I was curious. That curiosity cost me nothing. It didn't require an application fee or a credential or English fluency. It just required me to look up. This course is built on that same principle: your curiosity, your background, and your willingness to move forward are enough to start. The data I'm about to show you isn't meant to intimidate you. It's meant to show you where the industry is moving and why people like me, people from towns like mine, are exactly the people the world needs right now.

The Global Space Economy: A $770 Billion Reality

Let me be clear about something: space is not just NASA anymore. When I started my career, that was true. Most aerospace jobs were government or defense. Today, the industry looks completely different. In 2024, the global space economy reached $770 billion. That's not a projection. That's real money moving through real companies building real things. The growth rate is 9.2% per year. Over the next five years, that means the industry is roughly doubling in size.

Here's why this matters to you: a $770 billion industry doesn't run on hope. It runs on engineers. Thousands of them. The aerospace sector is projected to add 35,000 new positions in North America alone by 2030. But here's the part that changes everything: Latin American professionals fill only about 3% of these roles, despite making up 18% of the population. That gap isn't a sign that we're not capable. It's a sign that the industry hasn't looked hard enough. It's a sign of opportunity.

An entry-level aerospace engineer in the US makes more in a month than my mother made in a year when I was growing up. But it's not just the base salary. Companies like SpaceX, Blue Origin, and NASA offer signing bonuses from $15,000 to $50,000, stock options that actually vest, and relocation packages that remove barriers. For immigrants, this accelerates your path to financial independence, visa sponsorship, and permanent residency. This is real money that solves real problems.

The Pipeline Problem (Your Advantage)

The United States has one of the best engineering education systems in the world. And yet, aerospace has a severe shortage. Why? Because only 7.8% of US undergraduate engineers specialize in aerospace. Compare that to 18% in petroleum engineering or 22% in electrical engineering. Aerospace requires serious specialization: calculus, differential equations, physics, thermodynamics, control theory. Not every STEM student goes that deep. On top of that, many US engineers choose Silicon Valley over aerospace. The stock options feel bigger. The companies grow faster. The hype is louder. But aerospace doesn't have the same marketing budget. It doesn't need it. It needs engineers. And here's the part that's important: Latin American engineers often have more rigorous mathematical training than their US counterparts. Your education system pushed you harder on fundamentals. That's an advantage. When you combine that rigor with English fluency, you become a premium candidate. You're not competing with everyone. You're competing with the small percentage of people who chose aerospace and have your mathematical depth. That's a very small group.

Immigrant Engineers Win in Aerospace

There's actual research on this. MIT and Stanford have published studies on immigrant engineers in aerospace and defense. The findings are clear: teams led by immigrants file 36% more patents per employee. Companies with 25% or more immigrant technical staff ship products 2.3 times faster. Why? Because bilingual engineers understand supply chains across North America and Latin America. Because your perspective on problem-solving comes from different soil. Because you understand stakes in a way that gives you focus. NASA, SpaceX, Blue Origin, and Boeing all have diversity initiatives targeting Latin America. That's not charity. That's business. They know that diversity of background creates better engineering. You're not getting hired because of your story. You're getting hired because your story makes you a better engineer.

Where the Real Opportunities Are

Latin America is becoming infrastructure for global aerospace. Mexico has over 300 aerospace suppliers employing 60,000 workers. Boeing, Bombardier, and Airbus all source there. Brazil's space agency INPE launches satellites, and the commercial sector is growing fast. Argentina has a physics tradition that runs deep, and several leading SpaceX engineers are Argentine. Colombia is emerging in emerging technologies. The nearshoring effect is real: post-COVID supply chain restructuring is creating over 50,000 new engineering roles in Latin America by 2030. You have choices. You can stay in your country and build aerospace there, connecting to global companies. Or you can move to the US and build a career at NASA, SpaceX, or another Tier-1 company. Or you can do both, over time. The point is: unprecedented opportunities exist right now. This isn't a distant possibility. This is 2026. The hiring is happening today.

Video Lesson: My Path from Marripón to Mission Operations

Video Script (for Aracely)

I want to walk you through something personal before we go further into the data. When I was in Marripón, I didn't think of myself as someone who would work in space. I wasn't the kid with the NASA posters, though I should have been. I was the kid who was curious. And I was the kid who had two women at home showing me what it meant to work hard for something that mattered. My abuela didn't speak much. But she watched. And when I got interested in science, she found ways to support it. My mother worked hard. She made sure we had books. She made sure we had time to study. I spent hours on the porch in Marripón looking at the sky. I didn't know what I was looking at. I just knew it was there, and it was vast, and it was real. That curiosity was the beginning. Years later, I was in a karate competition in Peru. I was a black belt. I trained for years. And I learned something that karate taught me: discipline isn't about perfection. Discipline is about showing up every day and doing the work, even when you're not sure where it leads. Even when you're scared. Even when you fail. I failed a lot. When I applied to universities in the US, I was rejected from several. My English wasn't good enough, they said. I wasn't qualified enough, they said. But I kept going. I got a bachelor's degree. Then a master's. Then a PhD. Then another degree. Seven degrees total, earned while working, while learning English, while navigating a system that wasn't built for someone like me. And I kept moving forward. I worked on TRMM, the Tropical Rainfall Measuring Mission. I worked on LRO, the Lunar Reconnaissance Orbiter. I was part of the James Webb Space Telescope team before it launched on Christmas 2021. And now I lead operations on multiple NASA missions. I'm telling you this not because I'm special. I'm telling you this because I'm not. I'm someone from Marripón. I'm someone who looked up at the stars. I'm someone who had the support of two strong women and the discipline to show up. And if I can get here, you can get here too. The data in this lesson is important. The numbers matter. But the reason you're here, the reason you're watching this, is because you already know something: you can do this. You're just trying to figure out how. The rest of this course is how. Let's go.
