The Aracely Model: Track-Switching and Advancement

Dr. Aracely Quispe Neira began as an aerospace engineer, mastered systems on TRMM and LRO missions, led technical teams, and transitioned into Flight Operations to become a Flight Director. This progression was strategic:

  • Years 1-5: Deep engineering. Master subsystems. Debug real hardware. Build credibility.
  • Years 5-8: Lead engineer. Manage teams. Learn systems thinking. Bridge hardware and operations.
  • Years 8-12: Flight operations. Use technical credibility to make real decisions under pressure.
  • Years 12+: Director level. Manage entire missions. Interface with international partners.

Your first job does not define your career. Choose roles that build breadth. Each unlocks the next.

Video Script: Dr. Aracely Quispe Neira

"Hello. I'm Aracely. When I finished my engineering degree, I thought I knew my path. I would be an engineer for 30 years. Design spacecraft. Solve hard problems. That was the dream.

But something shifted around year five. I was leading a small team on a satellite mission. I realized I enjoyed managing the mission more than designing the spacecraft. I was good at it. My team listened. I could make decisions under pressure.

So I asked myself: Do I stay in engineering or move into operations? I was scared. But my manager told me something I never forgot. She said, 'Your expertise is your credibility. You will always have it. Operations is the next level. Try it.'

I moved into Mission Control. It was hard. Mission Control is where everything meets. Engineering. Operations. Safety. International partners. Real-time decisions. My first month, I made a mistake that delayed a pass by 90 seconds. I thought I was done. My flight director looked at me and said, 'Good. You made a mistake. Now you know why we have this procedure. You will never make it again. Teach the team what you learned.'

That is not how engineering culture works. In engineering, mistakes are hidden. In operations, mistakes become lessons for the entire team immediately. I loved it.

Today, I lead three missions. TRMM, LRO, JWST. And I tell young engineers: Do not lock into one path. Choose roles that teach you breadth. Ground test. Flight test. Operations. Program management. Each one makes you stronger for the next one.

And I tell young people from Latin America especially: You will face people who doubt you. Do not listen to them. I came here with $500 in my pocket and seven academic degrees I earned one at a time. I am here. You are here. The only person who can limit your path is you.

The course ends here. Your mission starts now. Good luck."

The 90-Day Career Exploration Roadmap

A question I get every week: "Aracely, I know I want to work in space, but I have no idea which path to choose. Where do I start?" Here is the answer I give. It worked for me. It has worked for every student I have mentored.

You do not choose a path by thinking. You choose it by doing. Give yourself 90 days to run four experiments, one every three weeks. At the end, the path will be obvious.

WeeksExperimentDeliverableWhat You Learn
1-3Build something small in the career area that scares you mostPublic GitHub repo or YouTube walkthroughIf you hate the work, you know fast. If you love it, you have your first portfolio piece.
4-6Do 5 informational interviews with people currently in that roleA private Notion page with notes from each callYou learn the day-to-day reality, not the LinkedIn fantasy. You get mentorship contacts.
7-9Apply to 10 internships or volunteer opportunities in that area10 applications submitted, tracked in a spreadsheetRejection teaches you what you are missing. Interviews teach you what they want.
10-12Publish a case study or technical write-up of what you learnedLinkedIn article, Medium post, or YouTube videoWriting forces clarity. Publishing builds your name. Recruiters read it.

Do this once. You will have a portfolio piece, five mentors, 10 application data points, and a publication. That is more than 90% of your competition has in their entire university career.

Lucía, Colombia, 29. Software engineer. Did the 90-day experiment on satellite operations. The first three weeks she built a Python tool that parsed TLE files and plotted ground tracks for any satellite by NORAD ID. The tool lives on GitHub with 340 stars today. In weeks 4-6 she emailed 15 satellite operators on LinkedIn. Five replied. Two became mentors. One of them introduced her to Planet Labs. She applied in week 7, interviewed in week 11, started in week 17. She is a Satellite Operations Engineer. Base $138,000. Equity grant worth approximately $80,000. Visa: O-1 (extraordinary ability) because she had the GitHub project and a published IEEE paper from her thesis.

Mateo, Mexico, 31. PhD in physics from UNAM. Did the experiment on orbital debris engineering. Weeks 1-3 he wrote a simulation of the Kessler effect in Julia. Weeks 4-6 he interviewed three engineers at LeoLabs, ExoAnalytic, and the Aerospace Corporation. Week 7 he applied to all three. He got four interviews, two offers. Chose LeoLabs in Menlo Park. Three real people. Same framework. Different paths. Every one of them said the hardest part was not the technical work. It was trusting that the framework would deliver results in 90 days when their friends told them they needed a PhD, five years of experience, and a perfect resume to even apply.

Visa Pathways by Career Track (for International Talent)

If you are reading this from outside the United States, the visa conversation is the most important one. I will be direct. Most of your LATAM mentors will not know the details of U.S. immigration law. Your university career office probably does not either. Here is what I have learned from my own paperwork and from mentoring 200+ engineers through this process.

VisaBest ForTypical TimelineKey Requirement
F-1 OPT / STEM OPTStudents finishing U.S. degreesApply 90 days before graduationCompleted U.S. degree in a STEM field

EB-1A / EB-1BSenior engineers, scientists, professors12-24 monthsPeer-reviewed publications, original contributions, citations
EB-2 NIWPhD or master's engineers whose work serves U.S. national interest18-36 monthsProve your work benefits the U.S. (space, climate, AI, biotech all qualify)
TN (Mexico only)Mexican engineers with a bachelor's in listed field1-2 weeks at borderNAFTA/USMCA professional category match

Three things I want you to hear clearly. First: aerospace is one of the only industries where O-1 and EB-2 NIW are achievable in the first three years of your career. Publish. Get press. File patents. That raises your ceiling before you even land. Second: if you are Mexican, the TN is a superpower. Use it. Third: never pay for a visa from your own pocket if the employer should pay. NASA pays. The big primes pay. NewSpace companies like SpaceX, Blue Origin, Planet, Rocket Lab pay. If an employer asks you to cover legal fees, that is a signal about how they will treat you for the next four years.

My Salary Negotiation Script (Use This Exactly)

Before I tell you the script, let me tell you why this matters. I left $35,000 on the table on my first offer because I did not know how to negotiate. My mentor at the time told me, "Aracely, they already decided they want you. The negotiation is not whether they hire you. It is how much they pay to keep you. You have more power than you think." Here is the exact sequence I now teach.

Step 1: Never name a number first. When the recruiter asks "What are your salary expectations?", say: "I want to make sure the role is the right fit first. Can you share the range you have budgeted for this position?" They will either name the range or push back. If they push back, repeat once. If they push back twice, say a number 20% above what you researched as market.

Step 2: When you get an offer, do not respond for 24 hours. Say: "Thank you so much. This is exciting. I want to review the full package with my family. Can we talk tomorrow?" Use that 24 hours to sleep, research, and write the counter.

Step 3: Counter with three asks, not one. Base salary, sign-on bonus, equity or relocation. Example: "Based on my research for similar roles at [comparable companies], and given that I bring [specific differentiator], I was hoping we could get to a base of $X, a sign-on of $Y to offset my moving expenses from Peru, and $Z in additional equity over the standard grant. Is there flexibility on any of these?"

Step 4: Silence is your friend. After you make the ask, stop talking. Do not justify. Do not apologize. Let them respond. The first person who speaks after a salary counter usually loses.

I have used this exact script in every negotiation since year five. I have added $220,000 in total compensation over my career just from 24 hours of preparation before accepting offers. You will too.

  • Pathfinders: The Golden Age of Arabic-Islamic Science — for perspective on how cultures that get pushed out of science find their way back in
  • The Right Stuff by Tom Wolfe — mythologizes astronaut culture, useful to understand the reference frame your U.S. colleagues grew up with

  • Space Generation Advisory Council (spacegeneration.org) — free student and young professional community, runs scholarships, mentorship programs, and international conferences
