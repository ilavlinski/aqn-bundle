The Aerospace Immigration Timeline: Reality Check

Most aerospace professionals from Latin America follow this trajectory: F-1 (2-3 years undergraduate or 2 years graduate) to OPT (12 months, or 36 months for STEM) to H-1B or EB green card sponsorship (5-10 years total from entry to citizenship). This is not a bug; it is the system design. Understanding the timeline reduces anxiety and helps you plan financially and psychologically.

I came on an extraordinary ability pathway. But most engineers you know follow the F-1 to H-1B to EB-2 sequence. That is the pathway. It requires patience. It requires discipline. It requires an immigration attorney. But it works.

OPT (Optional Practical Training): Your Post-Graduation Work Period

What is OPT? After you earn your bachelor's or master's degree on F-1, you can work in the United States for 12 months without an H-1B. This period is called OPT (Optional Practical Training). If your degree is in a STEM field (Science, Technology, Engineering, Mathematics), you can extend OPT for 24 additional months, totaling 36 months. This is one of the most valuable benefits of studying in the US.

OPT eligibility: You must have completed your degree and be authorized to work. Your school submits your OPT application to USCIS through a process called "cap-exempt." You file Form I-765 (Application for Employment Authorization Document) with your school's DSO (Designated School Official). The application costs $410 USD filing fee (as of 2025) and typically processes in 2-4 weeks.

STEM Extension: If your degree is in STEM (most aerospace, electrical, mechanical, software engineering degrees qualify), you are eligible for a 24-month extension. Your employer must support the extension by completing Form I-983 (Training Plan) showing you will receive formal training directly related to your field. The STEM OPT extension also requires that your employer use E-Verify (an employment verification system) and follow I-983 training protocols. Processing typically takes 2-3 months.

OPT work authorization terms: During OPT, you may work for any employer in your field. You do NOT need H-1B sponsorship. You are not locked to one company. However, you are bound by your authorized work period (12 months, or 36 months with STEM extension). After OPT expires, you must either return to your home country, enroll in further study (F-1 reset), or transition to H-1B or other visa sponsorship.

Critical detail: OPT is not a green card. It is temporary work authorization. Many students use the 12-36 month OPT period to gain US work experience, demonstrate expertise, build professional networks, and allow their employers to sponsor them for H-1B or EB-2 green card petitions. This is the strategic purpose of OPT.

Real Case Study: José Hernández, Migrant Farmworker to NASA Astronaut

José M. Hernández is the first migrant farmworker to become a NASA astronaut. He was born in the United States to parents who were field workers in California. Unlike Aracely, José is a US citizen by birth. But his story teaches us about persistence in aerospace careers.

José applied to the NASA astronaut program 11 times before being accepted. Each rejection was a redirection. He earned his PhD in electrical engineering from UC Santa Barbara while working. He flew on STS-128 in 2009 and has logged over 4,000 hours in space. His autobiography, "Reaching for the Stars," and the Netflix film "A Million Miles Away" tell his story.

Why mention José here? Because OPT and H-1B are tools for building the expertise NASA needs. José's pathway was different (US citizen), but the principle is the same: accumulate experience, earn credentials, demonstrate expertise, and the door opens. If you are on OPT working at Aerospace Corp, JPL, or Lockheed, you are doing exactly what José did: building unrefusable expertise.

H-1B Visa: The Specialty Occupation Lottery

What is H-1B? H-1B is a non-immigrant visa for specialty occupations. A specialty occupation requires a bachelor's degree or higher and involves theoretical and practical knowledge in highly specialized fields. Aerospace engineering qualifies. Software engineering qualifies. Data science qualifies. Nursing does not. Truck driving does not.

H-1B cap and lottery: The US government limits H-1B visas to 85,000 per fiscal year (October 1 to September 30): 65,000 regular cap and 20,000 US Masters exemption (H-1B visa holders with a US master's degree or higher are exempt from cap). In 2024, approximately 400,000 applications were filed for 85,000 slots. Your odds of selection in the regular lottery are roughly 20% (65,000 out of 400,000). If you have a US master's degree, the odds are better.

Critical caveat: These are approximate 2024 numbers. Odds fluctuate. Some recent years saw higher application volumes and worse odds. Always check current USCIS H-1B statistics.

H-1B cap-exempt employers: Some employers are exempt from H-1B caps. These include: universities and colleges, nonprofit research organizations, and government research institutions. If you work for a university, nonprofit research lab, or NASA Goddard (where I work), your employer can sponsor you for H-1B outside the cap lottery. Your odds of H-1B approval approach 100% if your employer chooses to sponsor you. This is one reason why NASA and research positions are valuable: cap-exempt sponsorship.

H-1B application process: Your employer files Form I-129 (Petition for Nonimmigrant Worker) with USCIS. If selected in the lottery (or cap-exempt), USCIS processes the petition. Processing typically takes 2-6 months. The petition costs approximately $1,500-$2,500 in USCIS fees and legal fees (if using an attorney, recommended). Your employer covers these costs in nearly all cases.

H-1B duration and renewal: H-1B status lasts 3 years. It can be renewed once for another 3 years, totaling 6 years maximum. During H-1B, you are locked to your sponsoring employer. Changing employers requires a new H-1B petition or employer transfer. You can have dependent family members (spouse, children under 21) join you on H-4 visas, though they cannot work unless on their own H-1B or other work visa.

H-1B to green card connection: H-1B is typically a stepping stone to permanent green card sponsorship. While on H-1B, your employer can sponsor you for an EB-2 (Employment-Based Second Preference) or EB-3 (Employment-Based Third Preference) green card. This process is called "green card sponsorship" or "PERM sponsorship" and is a separate lengthy process (2-5 years or longer).

Real Case Study: Franklin Chang-Díaz, Costa Rica to Harvard to Seven NASA Spaceflights

Franklin Chang-Díaz was born in San José, Costa Rica. He emigrated to the United States at age 18 with $50 in his pocket and spoke only Spanish. He earned his bachelor's degree in mechanical engineering from the University of Hartford and his PhD in plasma physics from MIT. He flew seven Space Shuttle missions (1986-2002), tied for the most spaceflights by any individual. He founded Ad Astra Rocket Company and is developing the VASIMR plasma engine for Mars missions.

Franklin's key quotes on immigration and aerospace: "My accent is not a barrier. My work is my voice." He talks openly about being rejected from programs, changing paths, and persisting. His story demonstrates that accent, language fluency, and even visa status are irrelevant if you produce excellent work.

Why mention Franklin? Because he represents the H-1B to green card to citizenship pathway. Franklin came through the immigration system legally, earned his expertise through US education, worked at NASA on H-1B (then permanent resident), and became a US citizen. He is now a successful entrepreneur. This is the pathway working.

O-1A Visa: Extraordinary Ability Alternative

What is O-1A? O-1A is a non-immigrant visa for individuals with extraordinary ability in their field. It is not as well-known as H-1B, but for some aerospace professionals, it is easier to qualify and does not involve a lottery.

O-1A extraordinary ability criteria (8 criteria; must meet at least 3):

  • Receipt of major national or international awards (Nobel Prize, MacArthur Fellowship, etc.)
  • Membership in associations that require outstanding achievement
  • Published material about you in professional publications
  • Evidence of original scientific, scholarly, or business-related contributions
  • Authorship of scholarly articles in professional journals or publications
  • Critical role in organizations/enterprises with high acclaim or recognition
  • Significant salary or remuneration reflecting extraordinary ability
  • Commercial success in the performing or creative arts

Can engineers qualify? Yes. You do NOT need a Nobel Prize. Here is a realistic breakdown:

  • Published research papers in peer-reviewed aerospace or engineering journals (satisfies "authorship of scholarly articles")
  • Patents issued in your name (satisfies "original contributions")
  • High salary offer or current salary (satisfies "significant salary")
  • Critical role in major aerospace projects or R&D (satisfies "critical role")
  • Membership in professional societies (IEEE, AIAA) (may satisfy "membership" if the society requires achievement)
  • Media coverage of your work (satisfies "published material about you")

O-1A cost and timeline: O-1A petitions cost approximately $2,000-$3,500 with attorney fees. Processing typically takes 3-6 months. O-1A is sponsored by your employer or by a petitioner (e.g., a law firm or advocacy organization). Unlike H-1B, there is no annual cap and no lottery. Every qualified case can be filed.

O-1A duration: O-1A status lasts 3 years and can be renewed indefinitely (as long as you maintain extraordinary ability). Like H-1B, you are typically locked to your sponsoring employer.

O-1A to green card: O-1A can transition to EB-1A (green card for extraordinary ability). This is a more direct path than H-1B to EB-2. However, EB-1A has higher bars than O-1A and typically requires a higher level of recognition. Many O-1A holders eventually pursue EB-2 NIW (National Interest Waiver) as a more achievable green card pathway.

Career Immigration Strategy: Your 5-10 Year Plan

Typical trajectory (conservative timeline):

  • Year 0-2: F-1 Undergraduate or Master's (or additional degree) - Study, build GPA, start research or internships, network with professors and advisors
  • Year 2-3: OPT (12 months) or STEM OPT (36 months) - Work for major aerospace employer (JPL, SpaceX, Blue Origin, Lockheed, Raytheon, etc.), gain practical experience, publish if possible, earn strong salary
  • Year 3-4: H-1B or O-1A filing - Your employer files H-1B (lottery in March) or O-1A (any time). Assuming success, you begin H-1B or O-1A status
  • Year 4-9: H-1B or O-1A employment + green card application - While on H-1B/O-1A, employer sponsors you for EB-2 green card. Processing takes 2-5 years or longer (depending on your country and employment category)
  • Year 9-10: Green card approval and employment-based green card receipt - You become permanent resident. Path to citizenship opens after 5 years of permanent residence

Accelerated trajectory (if you are cap-exempt): If you are hired by a university, government lab (NASA), or nonprofit research organization, H-1B filing is cap-exempt. You can file H-1B immediately after OPT, without lottery. This compresses the timeline to 3-4 years instead of 3-5.

EB-2 NIW trajectory (PhD holders): If you have a PhD or advanced degree and have done published research, you may qualify for EB-2 NIW (National Interest Waiver). This is a self-petitioning green card pathway that does not require employer sponsorship. It takes 1-3 years and costs less than employer-sponsored EB-2. For aerospace researchers with publication records, NIW is often faster than waiting for H-1B and EB-2 together.

Video Script: Dr. Aracely Quispe Neira, From OPT to Permanent Residence

Hello. After graduation, you face the biggest decision of your post-study path: where will you work, and what visa will sponsor you? Let me walk you through the realities.

OPT is your golden ticket. You have 12 months, or 36 months if your degree is STEM. Use this time strategically. Do not bounce between companies. Do not take a job just because it pays well. Take a job at a company that builds aerospace systems, because those companies have the resources and the motivation to sponsor your H-1B or green card later.

The major aerospace companies are: SpaceX, Blue Origin, Lockheed Martin, Raytheon (now RTX), Northrop Grumman, Boeing, General Dynamics. NASA contractors like Aerospace Corp, Axiom Space, Sierra Space. Research labs like NASA JPL, NASA Goddard, NASA Glenn. Universities with aerospace programs. Smaller companies: Vector Launch, Relativity Space, Rocket Lab (now a US company), and others.

Work at one of these companies during your OPT. Build expertise. Publish if you can. Help your manager understand your value. When your OPT is running down (usually in month 10-11 of your 12-month period, or month 34-35 of your 36-month period), your company should initiate H-1B sponsorship.

Here is how to position yourself: In your annual review or directly with your manager, say this: "I love working here. I want to continue contributing to this team. I know my F-1 OPT status will expire in [month]. I would like to discuss H-1B sponsorship for my continued employment." A company that values you will say yes.

If your employer says no, you have a problem. You have 60 days to change status or leave the US. This is serious. So pick your employer carefully during OPT. Pick a big company with HR departments that know how to sponsor visas.

Once your employer files H-1B, you are in the lottery (unless they are cap-exempt). The lottery is held in March for an October 1 start date. If you are selected, congratulations. If not, you have options: file again next year, pursue O-1A, or if you have advanced degree and research, pursue EB-2 NIW.

Let's say you get H-1B. You enter H-1B status on October 1. You can renew H-1B after 3 years for another 3 years, maximum 6 years. While on H-1B, your employer should begin green card sponsorship. Green card sponsorship is a separate process with its own timeline (1-5 years). You can be on H-1B and have a green card application pending simultaneously. This is normal and expected.

What if you do not get H-1B? If you are on STEM OPT (36 months), you have time to file again next year. If you are on regular OPT (12 months), you are in trouble if not selected. Consider:

One: File H-1B again next year if you have STEM OPT extension and time.

Two: Pursue O-1A if you have enough criteria (published papers, patents, high salary, critical role).

Three: Pursue EB-2 NIW if you have advanced degree and research.

Four: Return to your home country and reapply later as experienced professional.

The reality is: H-1B lottery rejection is painful, but it is not the end of your aerospace career. It is a redirect. Many aerospace engineers pursue O-1A instead and find it more stable (no annual lottery, indefinite renewals).

Final word: Get an immigration attorney as soon as you know you will graduate. Do not wait until OPT is expiring. Do not rely on your company's HR to understand your case. Your attorney is your advocate. Attorneys cost money, but the cost of making a visa mistake is deportation and years off your career.

You can do this. Thousands of international aerospace engineers are working in the US right now on H-1B, O-1A, and green cards. You will join them.

Knowledge Check Quiz (5 questions)

Q1: How many months of OPT does a US graduate with a STEM degree qualify for?
A) 12 months only | B) 24 months | C) 36 months total (12 base + 24 extension) | D) Unlimited
Answer: C) 36 months total (12 base + 24 extension). All F-1 graduates get 12 months of base OPT. STEM degree holders get an additional 24-month extension, totaling 36 months of work authorization.

Q2: Approximately how many H-1B visas are available annually, and how many applications are typically filed?
A) 20,000 available; 50,000 applications | B) 85,000 available; 400,000+ applications | C) 100,000 available; 200,000 applications | D) 50,000 available; 150,000 applications
Answer: B) 85,000 available; 400,000+ applications. The H-1B cap is 85,000 annually (65,000 regular + 20,000 US Masters exemption). In recent years, over 400,000 applications are filed, making the lottery competitive (roughly 20% odds).

Q3: Which employers are exempt from the H-1B cap lottery?
A) All tech companies | B) Universities, nonprofits, and government research institutions | C) Companies with over 500 employees | D) Aerospace companies only
Answer: B) Universities, nonprofits, and government research institutions. H-1B cap-exempt employers include colleges/universities, nonprofit research organizations, and government research institutions (like NASA). These can file H-1B outside the cap, giving virtually 100% approval odds.

Q4: How many of the 8 extraordinary ability criteria must you meet to qualify for O-1A?
A) All 8 | B) At least 6 | C) At least 3 | D) At least 1
Answer: C) At least 3. O-1A requires meeting at least 3 of the 8 extraordinary ability criteria. These can include published papers, patents, awards, critical employment roles, and significant salary.

Q5: Can you change employers while on H-1B status?
A) No, never | B) Yes, freely, like on OPT | C) Yes, but requires a new H-1B petition from the new employer | D) Only if approved by USCIS in advance
Answer: C) Yes, but requires a new H-1B petition from the new employer. While on H-1B, you are locked to your sponsoring employer. Changing employers requires the new employer to file a new H-1B petition and receive approval before you can work for them.
